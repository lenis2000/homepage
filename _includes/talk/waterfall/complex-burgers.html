The PDE Behind the Limit Shape

Euler–Lagrange equation [Cohn–Kenyon–Propp 2000]
\(\max \iint \sigma(\nabla h)\,dx\,dy\) yields \(\displaystyle \text{div}\,\nabla\sigma \circ \nabla h = 0\)
Complex Burgers equation [Kenyon–Okounkov 2007]
There exists a complex coordinate \(u=u(x,y)\) such that the Euler–Lagrange equation becomes
\(\displaystyle u_x = u \, u_y\)
the complex inviscid Burgers equation. The complex coordinate \(u(x,y)\) encodes the slope \(=\nabla h(x,y)\).
Arctic curve = shock of the complex Burgers equation
Where \(u\) becomes real, the complex conjugate solutions collide — this is the frozen boundary
Algebraic solutions: For a simply connected polygonal boundary, the arctic curve is a real algebraic curve inscribed in the domain
Beyond simply connected: Non-simply connected frozen boundaries arise from non-simply connected domains, or from "gas bubbles" (not present in the uniform lozenge case). A very active research direction
