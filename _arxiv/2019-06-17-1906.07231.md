{% raw %}<p>The domino-shuffling algorithm can be seen as a stochastic process describing the irreversible growth of a <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>2</mn><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(2+1)</annotation></semantics></math></span>-dimensional discrete interface. Its stationary speed of growth <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>v</mi><mi mathvariant="monospace">w</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">v_{\mathtt w}(\rho)</annotation></semantics></math></span> depends on the average interface slope <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ρ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rho</annotation></semantics></math></span>, as well as on the edge weights <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="monospace">w</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathtt w</annotation></semantics></math></span>, that are assumed to be periodic in space. We show that this growth model belongs to the Anisotropic KPZ class: one has <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>det</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mo stretchy="false">[</mo><msup><mi>D</mi><mn>2</mn></msup><msub><mi>v</mi><mi mathvariant="monospace">w</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo stretchy="false">]</mo><mo>&lt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\det [D^2 v_{\mathtt w}(\rho)]&lt;0</annotation></semantics></math></span> and the height fluctuations grow at most logarithmically in time. Moreover, we prove that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>D</mi><msub><mi>v</mi><mi mathvariant="monospace">w</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">D v_{\mathtt w}(\rho)</annotation></semantics></math></span> is discontinuous at each of the (finitely many) smooth (or "gaseous") slopes <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ρ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rho</annotation></semantics></math></span>; at these slopes, fluctuations do not diverge as time grows. For a special case of spatially <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>2</mn><mo>−</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2-</annotation></semantics></math></span>periodic weights, analogous results have been recently proven in Chhita-Toninelli (2018) via an explicit computation of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>v</mi><mi mathvariant="monospace">w</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">v_{\mathtt w}(\rho)</annotation></semantics></math></span>. In the general case, such a computation is out of reach; instead, our proof goes through a relation between the speed of growth and the limit shape of domino tilings of the Aztec diamond.</p>{% endraw %}
