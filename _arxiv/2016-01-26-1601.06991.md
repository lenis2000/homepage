{% raw %}<p>We study the length of cycles of random permutations drawn from the Mallows distribution. Under this distribution, the probability of a permutation <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>π</mi><mo>∈</mo><msub><mi mathvariant="double-struck">S</mi><mi>n</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\pi \in \mathbb{S}_n</annotation></semantics></math></span> is proportional to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>q</mi><mrow><mtext>inv</mtext><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>π</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q^{\textrm{inv}(\pi)}</annotation></semantics></math></span> where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>0</mn><mo>&lt;</mo><mi>q</mi><mo>≤</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">0&lt;q\le 1</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mtext>inv</mtext><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>π</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\textrm{inv}(\pi)</annotation></semantics></math></span> is the number of inversions in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>π</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\pi</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We show that the expected length of the cycle containing a given point is of order <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>min</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mo stretchy="false">{</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mi>q</mi><msup><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow></msup><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">}</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\min\{(1-q)^{-2}, n\}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. This marks the existence of two asymptotic regimes: with high probability, when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> tends to infinity with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mi>q</mi><msup><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow></msup><mo>≪</mo><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(1-q)^{-2} \ll n</annotation></semantics></math></span> then all cycles have size <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>o</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">o(n)</annotation></semantics></math></span> whereas when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> tends to infinity with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mi>q</mi><msup><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow></msup><mo>≫</mo><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(1-q)^{-2}\gg n</annotation></semantics></math></span> then macroscopic cycles, of size proportional to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span>, emerge. In the second regime, we prove that the distribution of normalized cycle lengths follows the Poisson-Dirichlet law, as in a uniformly random permutation. The results bear formal similarity with a conjectured localization transition for random band matrices. Further results are presented for the variance of the cycle lengths, the expected diameter of cycles and the expected number of cycles. The proofs rely on the exact sampling algorithm for the Mallows distribution and make use of a special diagonal exposure process for the graph of the permutation.</p>{% endraw %}
