{% raw %}<p>We introduce a general technique for proving estimates for certain random planar maps which belong to the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma</annotation></semantics></math></span>-Liouville quantum gravity (LQG) universality class for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>2</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma \in (0,2)</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The family of random planar maps we consider are those which can be encoded by a two-dimensional random walk with i.i.d.\ increments via a mating-of-trees bijection, and includes the uniform infinite planar triangulation (UIPT; <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>=</mo><msqrt><mrow><mn>8</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma=\sqrt{8/3}</annotation></semantics></math></span>); and planar maps weighted by the number of different spanning trees (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>=</mo><msqrt><mn>2</mn></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma=\sqrt 2</annotation></semantics></math></span>), bipolar orientations (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>=</mo><msqrt><mrow><mn>4</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma=\sqrt{4/3}</annotation></semantics></math></span>), or Schnyder woods (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma=1</annotation></semantics></math></span>) that can be put on the map. Using our technique, we prove estimates for graph distances in the above family of random planar maps. In particular, we obtain non-trivial upper and lower bounds for the cardinality of a graph distance ball consistent with the Watabiki (1993) prediction for the Hausdorff dimension of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma</annotation></semantics></math></span>-LQG and we establish the existence of an exponent for certain distances in the map. The basic idea of our approach is to compare a given random planar map <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>M</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">M</annotation></semantics></math></span> to a mated-CRT map---a random planar map constructed from a correlated two-dimensional Brownian motion---using a strong coupling (Zaitsev, 1998) of the encoding walk for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>M</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">M</annotation></semantics></math></span> and the Brownian motion used to construct the mated-CRT map. This allows us to deduce estimates for graph distances in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>M</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">M</annotation></semantics></math></span> from the estimates for graph distances in the mated-CRT map which we proved (using continuum theory) in a previous work. In the special case when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>=</mo><msqrt><mrow><mn>8</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma=\sqrt{8/3}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, we instead deduce estimates for the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msqrt><mrow><mn>8</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\sqrt{8/3}</annotation></semantics></math></span>-mated-CRT map from known results for the UIPT. The arguments of this paper do not directly use SLE/LQG, and can be read without any knowledge of these objects.</p>{% endraw %}
