We show that for a point-to-point polymer of length <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t</annotation></semantics></math></span> and any <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p\in (0,1)</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the quenched density of the point on the path which is <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi><mi>t</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">pt</annotation></semantics></math></span> distance away from the origin when centered around its random mode <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="script">M</mi><mrow><mi>p</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>t</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{M}_{p,t}</annotation></semantics></math></span> converges in law to an explicit random density function as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t\to\infty</annotation></semantics></math></span> without any scaling. Similarly, in the case of point-to-line polymers of length <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the quenched density of the endpoint of the path when centered around its random mode <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="script">M</mi><mrow><mo>∗</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>t</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{M}_{*,t}</annotation></semantics></math></span> converges in law to an explicit random density. The limiting random densities are proportional to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>e</mi><mrow><mo>−</mo><msub><mi mathvariant="script">R</mi><mi>σ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>x</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">e^{-\mathcal{R}_\sigma(x)}</annotation></semantics></math></span> where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="script">R</mi><mi>σ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>x</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{R}_\sigma(x)</annotation></semantics></math></span> is a two-sided 3D Bessel process with appropriate diffusion coefficient <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>σ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\sigma</annotation></semantics></math></span>. In addition, the laws of the random modes <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="script">M</mi><mrow><mo>∗</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>t</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{M}_{*,t}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="script">M</mi><mrow><mi>p</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>t</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{M}_{p,t}</annotation></semantics></math></span> themselves converge in distribution upon <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>t</mi><mrow><mn>2</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t^{2/3}</annotation></semantics></math></span> scaling to the maximizer of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">Airy</mi><mo>⁡</mo></mrow><mn>2</mn></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\operatorname{Airy}_2</annotation></semantics></math></span> process minus a parabola and points on the geodesics of the directed landscape respectively. Our localization results stated above provide an affirmative case of the folklore "favorite region" conjecture. Our proof techniques also allow us to prove properties of the KPZ equation such as ergodicity and limiting Bessel behaviors around the maximum.</p>{% endraw %}
