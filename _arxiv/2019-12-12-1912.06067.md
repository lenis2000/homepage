{% raw %}<p>Many integrable stochastic particle systems in one space dimension (such as TASEP - totally asymmetric simple exclusion process - and its various deformations, with a notable exception of ASEP) remain integrable when we equip each particle <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>x</mi><mi>i</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">x_i</annotation></semantics></math></span> with its own jump rate parameter <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>ν</mi><mi>i</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\nu_i</annotation></semantics></math></span>. It is a consequence of integrability that the distribution of each particle <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>x</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">x_n(t)</annotation></semantics></math></span> in a system started from the step initial configuration depends on the parameters <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>ν</mi><mi>j</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\nu_j</annotation></semantics></math></span>, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>j</mi><mo>≤</mo><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">j\le n</annotation></semantics></math></span>, in a symmetric way. A transposition <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>ν</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><mo>↔</mo><msub><mi>ν</mi><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\nu_n \leftrightarrow \nu_{n+1}</annotation></semantics></math></span> of the parameters thus affects only the distribution of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>x</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">x_n(t)</annotation></semantics></math></span>. For <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q</annotation></semantics></math></span>-Hahn TASEP and its degenerations (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q</annotation></semantics></math></span>-TASEP and directed beta polymer) we realize the transposition <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>ν</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><mo>↔</mo><msub><mi>ν</mi><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\nu_n \leftrightarrow \nu_{n+1}</annotation></semantics></math></span> as an explicit Markov swap operator acting on the single particle <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>x</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">x_n(t)</annotation></semantics></math></span>. For beta polymer, the swap operator can be interpreted as a simple modification of the lattice on which the polymer is considered. Our main tools are Markov duality and contour integral formulas for joint moments. In particular, our constructions lead to a continuous time Markov process <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi mathvariant="sans-serif">Q</mi><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi mathvariant="sans-serif">t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathsf{Q}^{(\mathsf{t})}</annotation></semantics></math></span> preserving the time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="sans-serif">t</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathsf{t}</annotation></semantics></math></span> distribution of the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q</annotation></semantics></math></span>-TASEP (with step initial configuration, where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="sans-serif">t</mi><mo>∈</mo><msub><mi mathvariant="double-struck">R</mi><mrow><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathsf{t}\in \mathbb{R}_{&gt;0}</annotation></semantics></math></span> is fixed). The dual system is a certain transient modification of the stochastic <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q</annotation></semantics></math></span>-Boson system. We identify asymptotic survival probabilities of this transient process with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q</annotation></semantics></math></span>-moments of the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q</annotation></semantics></math></span>-TASEP, and use this to show the convergence of the process <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi mathvariant="sans-serif">Q</mi><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi mathvariant="sans-serif">t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathsf{Q}^{(\mathsf{t})}</annotation></semantics></math></span> with arbitrary initial data to its stationary distribution. Setting <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q=0</annotation></semantics></math></span>, we recover the results about the usual TASEP established recently in [arXiv:1907.09155] by a different approach based on Gibbs ensembles of interlacing particles in two dimensions.</p>{% endraw %}
