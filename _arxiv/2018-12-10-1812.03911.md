{% raw %}<p>We consider the KPZ equation in space dimension 2 driven by space-time white noise. We showed in previous work that if the noise is mollified in space on scale <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ϵ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon</annotation></semantics></math></span> and its strength is scaled as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mover accent="true"><mi>β</mi><mo>^</mo></mover><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><msqrt><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi>ϵ</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi></mrow></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\hat\beta / \sqrt{|\log \epsilon|}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, then a transition occurs with explicit critical point <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mover accent="true"><mi>β</mi><mo>^</mo></mover><mi>c</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><msqrt><mrow><mn>2</mn><mi>π</mi></mrow></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\hat\beta_c = \sqrt{2\pi}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Recently Chatterjee and Dunlap showed that the solution admits subsequential scaling limits as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ϵ</mi><mo>↓</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon \downarrow 0</annotation></semantics></math></span>, for sufficiently small <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mover accent="true"><mi>β</mi><mo>^</mo></mover></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\hat\beta</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We prove here that the limit exists in the entire subcritical regime <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mover accent="true"><mi>β</mi><mo>^</mo></mover><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><msub><mover accent="true"><mi>β</mi><mo>^</mo></mover><mi>c</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\hat\beta \in (0, \hat\beta_c)</annotation></semantics></math></span> and we identify it as the solution of an additive Stochastic Heat Equation, establishing so-called Edwards-Wilkinson fluctuations. The same result holds for the directed polymer model in random environment in space dimension 2.</p>{% endraw %}
