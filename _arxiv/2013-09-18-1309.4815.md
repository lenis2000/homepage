{% raw %}<p>The circular law asserts that if <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>X</mi><mi>n</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">X_n</annotation></semantics></math></span> is a <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>×</mo><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n \times n</annotation></semantics></math></span> matrix with iid complex entries of mean zero and unit variance, then the empirical spectral distribution of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mfrac><mn>1</mn><msqrt><mi>n</mi></msqrt></mfrac><msub><mi>X</mi><mi>n</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} X_n</annotation></semantics></math></span> converges almost surely to the uniform distribution on the unit disk as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> tends to infinity. Answering a question of Tao, we prove the circular law for a general class of random block matrices with dependent entries. The proof relies on an inverse-type result for the concentration of linear operators and multilinear forms.</p>{% endraw %}
