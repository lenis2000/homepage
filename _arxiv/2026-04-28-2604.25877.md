{% raw %}<p>We study a natural analogue of Ulam's problem for random rooted trees distributed according to a Plancherel-type measure. This probability measure is closely related to the classical Plancherel measure on integer partitions. For a Plancherel random tree <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>T</mi><mi>n</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T_n</annotation></semantics></math></span> with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> vertices, we investigate the asymptotic behavior of its height <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>H</mi><mi>n</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">H_n</annotation></semantics></math></span>, defined as the maximal distance from the root to a leaf. We prove that this height grows logarithmically. More precisely, there is a one-parameter family of random trees <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>T</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>θ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>∈</mo><mi mathvariant="double-struck">N</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(T_n(\theta))_{n \in \mathbb{N}}</annotation></semantics></math></span> indexed by <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>θ</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\theta&gt;0</annotation></semantics></math></span> such that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mfrac><msub><mi>H</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><mrow><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi>n</mi></mrow></mfrac></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\frac{H_n}{\log n}</annotation></semantics></math></span> converges in probability to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>c</mi><mo>⋆</mo></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>θ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">c_\star(\theta)</annotation></semantics></math></span>, where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>c</mi><mo>⋆</mo></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>θ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">c_\star(\theta)</annotation></semantics></math></span> is an explicit constant depending on the parameter <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>θ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\theta</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The case of Plancherel trees corresponds to the parameter <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>θ</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\theta=2</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The proof is based on the fact that the Plancherel random trees can be viewed as Ewens fragmentation trees, for which the height exhibits a sharp threshold phenomenon. An upper bound is obtained via <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>s</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">s</annotation></semantics></math></span>-mass functionals and contraction estimates, while the lower bound is derived by embedding the model into a branching random walk with logarithmic displacements governed by a Poisson--Dirichlet distribution. The constant <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>c</mi><mo>⋆</mo></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>θ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">c_\star(\theta)</annotation></semantics></math></span> is characterized through a variational principle associated with this branching random walk.</p>{% endraw %}
