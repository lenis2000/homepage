When <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>V</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">V=0</annotation></semantics></math></span>, it is well-known that the unique invariant measure is the Brownian bridge. We further prove the measure has a finite relative entropy with respect to the law of the bridge and that, for any <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p\in(1,\infty)</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the corresponding Radon-Nikodym derivative belongs to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>L</mi><mi>p</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">L^p</annotation></semantics></math></span>, provided that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>∫</mo><msup><mi>V</mi><mn>2</mn></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\int V^2</annotation></semantics></math></span> is sufficiently small. The proof uses the discretization and mollification scheme of \cite{FQ}, together with an application of the log-Sobolev and spectral gap inequalities for the underlying Gaussian measure.</p>{% endraw %}
