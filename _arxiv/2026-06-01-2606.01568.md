{% raw %}<p>We introduce the \emph{tandem exclusion process} (TEP), a one-dimensional stochastic lattice model motivated by tandem running in ants. Particles evolve through two cooperative local transitions, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>110</mn><mo>→</mo><mn>101</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">110\to101</annotation></semantics></math></span> at rate <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>α</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\alpha</annotation></semantics></math></span> (leader advancement) and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>101</mn><mo>→</mo><mn>011</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">101\to011</annotation></semantics></math></span> at rate <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>β</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\beta</annotation></semantics></math></span> (follower recovery). We prove that the stationary measure on the dynamically active sector is the Gibbs measure <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>π</mi><mo>∝</mo><msup><mi>q</mi><mrow><mi>H</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>η</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\pi \propto q^{H(\eta)}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>β</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mi>α</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q=\beta/\alpha</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>H</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>η</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">H(\eta)</annotation></semantics></math></span> counts neighboring occupied pairs, and derive exact closed-form expressions for the stationary current and spatial correlations using transfer-matrix methods. The current is asymmetric under particle--hole exchange <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ρ</mi><mo>↦</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mi>ρ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rho \mapsto1-\rho</annotation></semantics></math></span>, with its maximum occurring at densities strictly larger than <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/2</annotation></semantics></math></span>, in contrast to the symmetric current <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rho(1-\rho)</annotation></semantics></math></span> of the totally asymmetric simple exclusion process (TASEP). For <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q&gt;1</annotation></semantics></math></span>, cooperative dynamics enhances the current above the TASEP value and generates strong spatial clustering; in the limit <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q\to\infty</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the current approaches <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>J</mi><mo>→</mo><mi>α</mi><mi>ρ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">J\to\alpha \rho</annotation></semantics></math></span>, corresponding to nearly unconstrained collective transport. These results suggest that tandem coordination alone can substantially enhance collective transport efficiency at moderate and high densities, even without pheromone-mediated long-range communication.</p>{% endraw %}
