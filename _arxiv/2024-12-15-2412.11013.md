{% raw %}<p>The quasisymmetric functions, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>Q</mi><mi>S</mi><mi>y</mi><mi>m</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">QSym</annotation></semantics></math></span>, are generalized for a finite alphabet <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> by the colored quasisymmetric functions, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>Q</mi><mi>S</mi><mi>y</mi><msub><mi>m</mi><mi>A</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">QSym_A</annotation></semantics></math></span>, in partially commutative variables. Their dual, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi><mi>S</mi><mi>y</mi><msub><mi>m</mi><mi>A</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">NSym_A</annotation></semantics></math></span>, generalizes the noncommutative symmetric functions, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi><mi>S</mi><mi>y</mi><mi>m</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">NSym</annotation></semantics></math></span>, through a relationship with a Hopf algebra of trees. We define an algebra <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>S</mi><mi>y</mi><msub><mi>m</mi><mi>A</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Sym_A</annotation></semantics></math></span>, contained within <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>Q</mi><mi>S</mi><mi>y</mi><msub><mi>m</mi><mi>A</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">QSym_A</annotation></semantics></math></span>, that is isomorphic to the symmetric functions, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>S</mi><mi>y</mi><mi>m</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Sym</annotation></semantics></math></span>, when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> is an alphabet of size one. We show that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>S</mi><mi>y</mi><msub><mi>m</mi><mi>A</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Sym_A</annotation></semantics></math></span> is a Hopf algebra and define its graded dual, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mi>S</mi><mi>y</mi><msub><mi>m</mi><mi>A</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">PSym_A</annotation></semantics></math></span>, which is the commutative image of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi><mi>S</mi><mi>y</mi><msub><mi>m</mi><mi>A</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">NSym_A</annotation></semantics></math></span> and also generalizes <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>S</mi><mi>y</mi><mi>m</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Sym</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The seven algebras listed here can be placed in a commutative diagram connected by Hopf morphisms. In addition to defining generalizations of the classic bases of the symmetric functions to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>S</mi><mi>y</mi><msub><mi>m</mi><mi>A</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Sym_A</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mi>S</mi><mi>y</mi><msub><mi>m</mi><mi>A</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">PSym_A</annotation></semantics></math></span>, we describe multiplication, comultiplication, and the antipode in terms of a basis for both algebras. We conclude by defining a pair of dual bases that generalize the Schur functions and listing open questions.</p>{% endraw %}
