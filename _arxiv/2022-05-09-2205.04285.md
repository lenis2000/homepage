In this article, we prove a central limit theorem for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>T</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>H</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><msub><mi>G</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T(H,G_n)</annotation></semantics></math></span> with explicit error rates. The error rates arise from graph counts of collections formed by joining copies of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>H</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">H</annotation></semantics></math></span> that we call good joins. Counts of good joins are closely related to the fourth moment of a normalized version of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>T</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>H</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><msub><mi>G</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T(H,G_{n})</annotation></semantics></math></span>, and that connection allows us to show a fourth moment phenomenon for the central limit theorem. Precisely, for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>c</mi><mo>≥</mo><mn>30</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">c\ge 30</annotation></semantics></math></span>, we show that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>T</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>H</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><msub><mi>G</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T(H,G_n)</annotation></semantics></math></span> (appropriately centered and rescaled) converges in distribution to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">N</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{N}(0,1)</annotation></semantics></math></span> whenever its fourth moment converges to 3 (the fourth moment of the standard normal distribution). We show the convergence of the fourth moment is necessary to obtain a normal limit when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>c</mi><mo>≥</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">c\ge 2</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The combination of these results implies that the fourth moment condition characterizes the limiting normal distribution of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>T</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>H</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><msub><mi>G</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T(H,G_n)</annotation></semantics></math></span> for all subgraphs <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>H</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">H</annotation></semantics></math></span>, whenever <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>c</mi><mo>≥</mo><mn>30</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">c\ge 30</annotation></semantics></math></span>.</p>{% endraw %}
