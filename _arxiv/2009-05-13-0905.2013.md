{% raw %}<p>We prove a formula for the number of permutations in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>S</mi><mi>n</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">S_n</annotation></semantics></math></span> such that their first <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>−</mo><mi>k</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n-k</annotation></semantics></math></span> entries are increasing and their longest increasing subsequence has length <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>−</mo><mi>k</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n-k</annotation></semantics></math></span>. This formula first appeared as a consequence of character polynomial calculations in recent work of Adriano Garsia and Alain Goupil. We give two `elementary' bijective proofs of this result and of its <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q</annotation></semantics></math></span>-analogue, one proof using the RSK correspondence and one only permutations.</p>{% endraw %}
