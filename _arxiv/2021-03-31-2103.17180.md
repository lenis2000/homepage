{% raw %}<p>Suppose that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>m</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">m</annotation></semantics></math></span> drivers each choose a preferred parking space in a linear car park with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> spots. In order, each driver goes to their chosen spot and parks there if possible, and otherwise takes the next available spot if it exists. If all drivers park successfully, the sequence of choices is called a parking function. Classical parking functions correspond to the case <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>m</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">m=n</annotation></semantics></math></span>; we study here combinatorial and probabilistic aspects of this generalized case. We construct a family of bijections between parking functions <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mtext>PF</mtext><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>m</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\text{PF}(m, n)</annotation></semantics></math></span> with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>m</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">m</annotation></semantics></math></span> cars and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> spots and spanning forests <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">F</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>n</mi><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mi>m</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathscr{F}(n+1, n+1-m)</annotation></semantics></math></span> with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n+1</annotation></semantics></math></span> vertices and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mi>m</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n+1-m</annotation></semantics></math></span> distinct trees having specified roots. This leads to a bijective correspondence between <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mtext>PF</mtext><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>m</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\text{PF}(m, n)</annotation></semantics></math></span> and monomial terms in the associated Tutte polynomial of a disjoint union of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>−</mo><mi>m</mi><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n-m+1</annotation></semantics></math></span> complete graphs. We present an identity between the "inversion enumerator" of spanning forests with fixed roots and the "displacement enumerator" of parking functions. The displacement is then related to the number of graphs on <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n+1</annotation></semantics></math></span> labeled vertices with a fixed number of edges, where the graph has <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mi>m</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n+1-m</annotation></semantics></math></span> disjoint rooted components with specified roots. We investigate various probabilistic properties of a uniform parking function, giving a formula for the law of a single coordinate. As a side result we obtain a recurrence relation for the displacement enumerator. Adapting known results on random linear probes, we further deduce the covariance between two coordinates when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>m</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">m=n</annotation></semantics></math></span>.</p>{% endraw %}
