The distribution of the largest eigenvalue of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>A</mi><mo>+</mo><mi>B</mi><msup><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow></msup><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(A+B)^{-1}B</annotation></semantics></math></span> has numerous applications in multivariate statistics, but is difficult to calculate exactly. Suppose that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>m</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">m</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> grow in proportion to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We show that after centering and scaling, the distribution is approximated to second-order, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>O</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msup><mi>p</mi><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>2</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">O(p^{-2/3})</annotation></semantics></math></span>, by the Tracy--Widom law. Asymptotic approximations of Jacobi polynomials near the largest zero play a central role.</p>{% endraw %}
