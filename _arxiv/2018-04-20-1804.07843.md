{% raw %}<p>In last passage percolation models, the energy of a path is maximized over all directed paths with given endpoints in a random environment, and the maximizing paths are called geodesics. The geodesics and their energy can be scaled so that transformed geodesics cross unit distance and have fluctuations and scaled energy of unit order. Here we consider Poissonian last passage percolation, a model lying in the KPZ universality class, and refer to scaled geodesics as polymers and their scaled energies as weights. Polymers may be viewed as random functions of the vertical coordinate and, when they are, we show that they have modulus of continuity whose order is at most <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>t</mi><mrow><mn>2</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup><mo fence="false" stretchy="true" minsize="1.2em" maxsize="1.2em">(</mo><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><msup><mi>t</mi><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow></msup><msup><mo fence="false" stretchy="true" minsize="1.2em" maxsize="1.2em">)</mo><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t^{2/3}\big(\log t^{-1}\big)^{1/3}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The power of one-third in the logarithm may be expected to be sharp and in a related problem we show that it is: among polymers in the unit box whose endpoints have vertical separation <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t</annotation></semantics></math></span> (and a horizontal separation of the same order), the maximum transversal fluctuation has order <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>t</mi><mrow><mn>2</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup><mo fence="false" stretchy="true" minsize="1.2em" maxsize="1.2em">(</mo><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><msup><mi>t</mi><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow></msup><msup><mo fence="false" stretchy="true" minsize="1.2em" maxsize="1.2em">)</mo><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t^{2/3}\big(\log t^{-1}\big)^{1/3}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. In this way, we identify exponent pairs of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>2</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(2/3,1/3)</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>2</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(1/3,2/3)</annotation></semantics></math></span> in power law and polylogarithmic correction, respectively for polymer fluctuation, and polymer weight under vertical endpoint perturbation.
