{% raw %}<p>Recently, there has been significant interest in applying the method of moments developed by Wood and others to study distributions of finite abelian groups that arise in number theory and combinatorics. When the moments do not grow too fast, they determine a unique distribution. We construct large families of distributions that have the same moments. These families include several distributions that arise naturally in the study of sandpile groups of families of random graphs. Wood determined the distribution of Sylow <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p</annotation></semantics></math></span>-subgroups of sandpile groups of Erdős--Rényi random graphs. This was extended by Mészáros to sandpile groups of random <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d</annotation></semantics></math></span>-regular graphs, who observed an interesting special case when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d</annotation></semantics></math></span> is even and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p = 2</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We study Sylow <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p</annotation></semantics></math></span>-subgroups of sandpile groups of random bipartite graphs and similarly find a special case for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p =2</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Although this distribution differs from that of Mészáros, we show that they have the same moments and fit into our broader construction. To compute the moments of the distributions we study, we apply combinatorial tools from the theory of Hall--Littlewood functions.</p>{% endraw %}
