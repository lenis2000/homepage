{% raw %}<p>A homomorphism height function on the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d</annotation></semantics></math></span>-dimensional torus <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">Z</mi><mi>n</mi><mi>d</mi></msubsup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{Z}_n^d</annotation></semantics></math></span> is a function taking integer values on the vertices of the torus with consecutive integers assigned to adjacent vertices. A Lipschitz height function is defined similarly but may also take equal values on adjacent vertices. In each model, we consider the uniform distribution over such functions, subject to boundary conditions. We prove that in high dimensions, with zero boundary values, a typical function is very flat, having bounded variance at any fixed vertex and taking at most <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>C</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi>n</mi><msup><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mi>d</mi></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">C(\log n)^{1/d}</annotation></semantics></math></span> values with high probability. Our results extend to any dimension <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>≥</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d\ge 2</annotation></semantics></math></span>, if <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">Z</mi><mi>n</mi><mi>d</mi></msubsup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{Z}_n^d</annotation></semantics></math></span> is replaced by an enhanced version of it, the torus <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">Z</mi><mi>n</mi><mi>d</mi></msubsup><mo>×</mo><msubsup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">Z</mi><mn>2</mn><msub><mi>d</mi><mn>0</mn></msub></msubsup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{Z}_n^d\times\mathbb{Z}_2^{d_0}</annotation></semantics></math></span> for some fixed <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>d</mi><mn>0</mn></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d_0</annotation></semantics></math></span>. This establishes one side of a conjectured roughening transition in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2</annotation></semantics></math></span> dimensions. The full transition is established for a class of tori with non-equal side lengths. We also find that when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d</annotation></semantics></math></span> is taken to infinity while <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> remains fixed, a typical function takes at most <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>r</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">r</annotation></semantics></math></span> values with high probability, where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>r</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>5</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">r=5</annotation></semantics></math></span> for the homomorphism model and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>r</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>4</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">r=4</annotation></semantics></math></span> for the Lipschitz model. Suitable generalizations are obtained when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> grows with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Our results apply also to the related model of uniform 3-coloring and establish, for certain boundary conditions, that a uniformly sampled proper 3-coloring of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">Z</mi><mi>n</mi><mi>d</mi></msubsup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{Z}_n^d</annotation></semantics></math></span> will be nearly constant on either the even or odd sub-lattice. Our proofs are based on a combinatorial transformation and on a careful analysis of the properties of a class of cutsets which we term odd cutsets. For the Lipschitz model, our results rely also on a bijection of Yadin. This work generalizes results of Galvin and Kahn, refutes a conjecture of Benjamini, Yadin and Yehudayoff and answers a question of Benjamini, H\"aggstr\"om and Mossel.</p>{% endraw %}
