{% raw %}<p>There is a simple way to "glue together" a coupled pair of continuum random trees (CRTs) to produce a topological sphere. The sphere comes equipped with a measure and a space-filling curve (which describes the "interface" between the trees). We present an explicit and canonical way to embed the sphere in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="bold">C</mi><mo>∪</mo><mo stretchy="false">{</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi><mo stretchy="false">}</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\mathbf C} \cup \{ \infty \}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. In this embedding, the measure is Liouville quantum gravity (LQG) with parameter <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>2</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma \in (0,2)</annotation></semantics></math></span>, and the curve is space-filling SLE<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><msup><mi>κ</mi><mo mathvariant="normal" lspace="0em" rspace="0em">′</mo></msup></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_{\kappa&#x27;}</annotation></semantics></math></span> with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>κ</mi><mo mathvariant="normal" lspace="0em" rspace="0em">′</mo></msup><mo>=</mo><mn>16</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><msup><mi>γ</mi><mn>2</mn></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\kappa&#x27; = 16/\gamma^2</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Achieving this requires us to develop an extensive suite of tools for working with LQG surfaces. We explain how to conformally weld so-called "quantum wedges" to obtain new quantum wedges of different weights. We construct finite-volume quantum disks and spheres of various types, and give a Poissonian description of the set of quantum disks cut off by a boundary-intersecting SLE<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><mi>κ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_{\kappa}(\rho)</annotation></semantics></math></span> process with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>κ</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>4</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\kappa \in (0,4)</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We also establish a L\'evy tree description of the set of quantum disks to the left (or right) of an SLE<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><msup><mi>κ</mi><mo mathvariant="normal" lspace="0em" rspace="0em">′</mo></msup></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_{\kappa&#x27;}</annotation></semantics></math></span> with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>κ</mi><mo mathvariant="normal" lspace="0em" rspace="0em">′</mo></msup><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>4</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>8</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\kappa&#x27; \in (4,8)</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We show that given two such trees, sampled independently, there is a.s. a canonical way to "zip them together" and recover the SLE<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><msup><mi>κ</mi><mo mathvariant="normal" lspace="0em" rspace="0em">′</mo></msup></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_{\kappa&#x27;}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The law of the CRT pair we study was shown in an earlier paper to be the scaling limit of the discrete tree/dual-tree pair associated to an FK-decorated random planar map (RPM). Together, these results imply that FK-decorated RPM scales to CLE-decorated LQG in a certain "tree structure" topology.</p>{% endraw %}
