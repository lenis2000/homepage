{% raw %}<p>Stochastic growth processes in dimension <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>2</mn><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(2+1)</annotation></semantics></math></span> were conjectured by D. Wolf, on the basis of renormalization-group arguments, to fall into two distinct universality classes, according to whether the Hessian <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>H</mi><mi>ρ</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">H_\rho</annotation></semantics></math></span> of the speed of growth <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>v</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">v(\rho)</annotation></semantics></math></span> as a function of the average slope <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ρ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rho</annotation></semantics></math></span> satisfies <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>det</mi><mo>⁡</mo><msub><mi>H</mi><mi>ρ</mi></msub><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\det H_\rho&gt;0</annotation></semantics></math></span> ("isotropic KPZ class") or <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>det</mi><mo>⁡</mo><msub><mi>H</mi><mi>ρ</mi></msub><mo>≤</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\det H_\rho\le 0</annotation></semantics></math></span> ("anisotropic KPZ (AKPZ)" class). The former is characterized by strictly positive growth and roughness exponents, while in the AKPZ class fluctuations are logarithmic in time and space. It is natural to ask (a) if one can exhibit interesting growth models with "smooth" stationary states, i.e., with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>O</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">O(1)</annotation></semantics></math></span> fluctuations (instead of logarithmically or power-like growing, as in Wolf's picture) and (b) what new phenomena arise when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>v</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mo>⋅</mo><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">v(\cdot)</annotation></semantics></math></span> is not smooth, so that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>H</mi><mi>ρ</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">H_\rho</annotation></semantics></math></span> is not defined. The two questions are actually related and here we provide an answer to both, in a specific framework. We define a <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>2</mn><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(2+1)</annotation></semantics></math></span>-dimensional interface growth process, based on the so-called shuffling algorithm for domino tilings. The stationary, non-reversible measures are translation-invariant Gibbs measures on perfect matchings of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">Z</mi><mn>2</mn></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb Z^2</annotation></semantics></math></span>, with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2</annotation></semantics></math></span>-periodic weights. If <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ρ</mi><mo mathvariant="normal">≠</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rho\ne0</annotation></semantics></math></span>, fluctuations are known to grow logarithmically in space and to behave like a two-dimensional GFF. We prove that fluctuations grow at most logarithmically in time and that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>det</mi><mo>⁡</mo><msub><mi>H</mi><mi>ρ</mi></msub><mo>&lt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\det H_\rho&lt;0</annotation></semantics></math></span>: the model belongs to the AKPZ class. When <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ρ</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rho=0</annotation></semantics></math></span>, instead, the stationary state is "smooth", with correlations uniformly bounded in space and time; correspondingly, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>v</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mo>⋅</mo><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">v(\cdot)</annotation></semantics></math></span> is not differentiable at <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ρ</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rho=0</annotation></semantics></math></span> and we extract the singularity of the eigenvalues of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>H</mi><mi>ρ</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">H_\rho</annotation></semantics></math></span> for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ρ</mi><mo>∼</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rho\sim 0</annotation></semantics></math></span>.</p>{% endraw %}
