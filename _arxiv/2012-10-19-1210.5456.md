{% raw %}<p>TThe prototypical problem we study here is the following. Given a <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>2</mn><mi>L</mi><mo>×</mo><mn>2</mn><mi>L</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2L\times 2L</annotation></semantics></math></span> square, there are approximately <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>exp</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>4</mn><mi>K</mi><msup><mi>L</mi><mn>2</mn></msup><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mi>π</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\exp(4KL^2/\pi )</annotation></semantics></math></span> ways to tile it with dominos, i.e. with horizontal or vertical <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>2</mn><mo>×</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2\times 1</annotation></semantics></math></span> rectangles, where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>K</mi><mo>≈</mo><mn>0.916</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">K\approx 0.916</annotation></semantics></math></span> is Catalan's constant [Kasteleyn '61, Temperley-Fisher '61]. A conceptually simple (even if computationally not the most efficient) way of sampling uniformly one among so many tilings is to introduce a Markov Chain algorithm (Glauber dynamics) where, with rate <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1</annotation></semantics></math></span>, two adjacent horizontal dominos are flipped to vertical dominos, or vice-versa. The unique invariant measure is the uniform one and a classical question [Wilson 2004,Luby-Randall-Sinclair 2001] is to estimate the time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>T</mi><mrow><mi>m</mi><mi>i</mi><mi>x</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T_{mix}</annotation></semantics></math></span> it takes to approach equilibrium (i.e. the running time of the algorithm). In [Luby-Randall-Sinclair 2001, Randall-Tetali 2000], fast mixin was proven: <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>T</mi><mrow><mi>m</mi><mi>i</mi><mi>x</mi></mrow></msub><mo>=</mo><mi>O</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msup><mi>L</mi><mi>C</mi></msup><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T_{mix}=O(L^C)</annotation></semantics></math></span> for some finite <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>C</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">C</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Here, we go much beyond and show that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>c</mi><msup><mi>L</mi><mn>2</mn></msup><mo>≤</mo><msub><mi>T</mi><mrow><mi>m</mi><mi>i</mi><mi>x</mi></mrow></msub><mo>≤</mo><msup><mi>L</mi><mrow><mn>2</mn><mo>+</mo><mi>o</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">c L^2\le T_{mix}\le L^{2+o(1)}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Our result applies to rather general domain shapes (not just the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>2</mn><mi>L</mi><mo>×</mo><mn>2</mn><mi>L</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2L\times 2L</annotation></semantics></math></span> square), provided that the typical height function associated to the tiling is macroscopically planar in the large <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>L</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">L</annotation></semantics></math></span> limit, under the uniform measure (this is the case for instance for the Temperley-type boundary conditions considered in [Kenyon 2000]). Also, our method extends to some other types of tilings of the plane, for instance the tilings associated to dimer coverings of the hexagon or square-hexagon lattices.</p>{% endraw %}
