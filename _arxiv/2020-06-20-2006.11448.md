{% raw %}<p>In discrete planar last passage percolation (LPP), random values are assigned independently to each vertex in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">Z</mi><mn>2</mn></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb Z^2</annotation></semantics></math></span>, and each finite upright path in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">Z</mi><mn>2</mn></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb Z^2</annotation></semantics></math></span> is ascribed the weight given by the sum of values of its vertices. The weight of a collection of disjoint paths is the sum of its members' weights. The notion of a geodesic, a maximum weight path between two vertices, has a natural generalization concerning several disjoint paths: a <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>k</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k</annotation></semantics></math></span>-geodesic watermelon in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">[</mo><mn>1</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>n</mi><msup><mo stretchy="false">]</mo><mn>2</mn></msup><mo>∩</mo><msup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">Z</mi><mn>2</mn></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">[1,n]^2\cap\mathbb Z^2</annotation></semantics></math></span> is a collection of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>k</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k</annotation></semantics></math></span> disjoint paths contained in this square that has maximum weight among all such collections. While the weights of such collections are known to be important objects, the maximizing paths have been largely unexplored beyond the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>k</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k=1</annotation></semantics></math></span> case. For exactly solvable models, such as exponential and geometric LPP, it is well known that for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>k</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k=1</annotation></semantics></math></span> the exponents that govern fluctuation in weight and transversal distance are <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/3</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>2</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2/3</annotation></semantics></math></span>; that is, typically, the weight of the geodesic on the route <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>→</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(1,1) \to (n,n)</annotation></semantics></math></span> fluctuates around a dominant linear growth of the form <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>μ</mi><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mu n</annotation></semantics></math></span> by the order of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>n</mi><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n^{1/3}</annotation></semantics></math></span>; and the maximum Euclidean distance of the geodesic from the diagonal has order <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>n</mi><mrow><mn>2</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n^{2/3}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Assuming a strong but local form of convexity and one-point moderate deviation bounds for the geodesic weight profile---which are available in all known exactly solvable models---we establish that, typically, the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>k</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k</annotation></semantics></math></span>-geodesic watermelon's weight falls below <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>μ</mi><mi>n</mi><mi>k</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mu nk</annotation></semantics></math></span> by order <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>k</mi><mrow><mn>5</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup><msup><mi>n</mi><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k^{5/3}n^{1/3}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, and its transversal fluctuation is of order <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>k</mi><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup><msup><mi>n</mi><mrow><mn>2</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k^{1/3}n^{2/3}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Our arguments crucially rely on, and develop, a remarkable deterministic interlacing property that the watermelons admit. Our methods also yield sharp rigidity estimates for naturally associated point processes, which improve on estimates obtained via tools from the theory of determinantal point processes available in the integrable setting.</p>{% endraw %}
