{% raw %}<p>We consider TASEP with two types of particles starting at every second site. Particles to the left of the origin have jump rate <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1</annotation></semantics></math></span>, while particles to the right have jump rate <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>α</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\alpha</annotation></semantics></math></span>. When <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>α</mi><mo>&lt;</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\alpha&lt;1</annotation></semantics></math></span> there is a formation of a shock where the density jumps to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mi>α</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(1-\alpha)/2</annotation></semantics></math></span>. For <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>α</mi><mo>&lt;</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\alpha&lt;1</annotation></semantics></math></span> fixed, the statistics of the associated height functions around the shock is asymptotically (as time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t\to\infty</annotation></semantics></math></span>) a maximum of two independent random variables as shown in\cite{FN14}. In this paper we consider the critical scaling when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mi>α</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>a</mi><msup><mi>t</mi><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1-\alpha=a t^{-1/3}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi><mo>≫</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t\gg 1</annotation></semantics></math></span> is the observation time. In that case the decoupling does not occur anymore. We determine the limiting distributions of the shock and numerically study its convergence as a function of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>a</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">a</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We see that the convergence to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi>F</mi><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">G</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">O</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">E</mi></mrow><mn>2</mn></msubsup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">F_{\rm GOE}^2</annotation></semantics></math></span> occurs quite rapidly as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>a</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">a</annotation></semantics></math></span> increases. The critical scaling is analogue to the one used in the last passage percolation to obtain the BBP transition processes\cite{BBP06}.</p>{% endraw %}
