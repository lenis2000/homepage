{% raw %}<p>We show that for any semi-random transposition shuffle on <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> cards, the mixing time of any given <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>k</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k</annotation></semantics></math></span> cards is at most <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi>k</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n\log k</annotation></semantics></math></span>, provided <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>k</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>o</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi>n</mi><msup><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow></msup><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k=o((n/\log n)^{1/2})</annotation></semantics></math></span>. In the case of the top-to-random transposition shuffle we show that there is cutoff at this time with a window of size O(n), provided further that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>k</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k\to\infty</annotation></semantics></math></span> as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n\to\infty</annotation></semantics></math></span> (and no cutoff otherwise). For the random-to-random transposition shuffle we show cutoff at time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mi>n</mi><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi>k</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(1/2)n\log k</annotation></semantics></math></span> for the same conditions on <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>k</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Finally, we analyse the cyclic-to-random transposition shuffle and show partial mixing occurs at time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>≤</mo><mi>α</mi><mi>n</mi><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi>k</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\le\alpha n\log k</annotation></semantics></math></span> for some <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>α</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\alpha</annotation></semantics></math></span> just larger than 1/2. We prove these results by relating the mixing time of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>k</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k</annotation></semantics></math></span> cards to the mixing of one card. Our results rely heavily on coupling arguments to bound the total variation distance.</p>{% endraw %}
