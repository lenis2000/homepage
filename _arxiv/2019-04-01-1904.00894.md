{% raw %}<p>The classical theorem by Pitman states that a Brownian motion minus twice its running infimum enjoys the Markov property. On the one hand, Biane understood that Pitman's theorem is intimately related to the representation theory of the quantum group <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="script">U</mi><mi>q</mi></msub><mrow><mo fence="true">(</mo><msub><mrow><mi mathvariant="fraktur">s</mi><mi mathvariant="fraktur">l</mi></mrow><mn>2</mn></msub><mo fence="true">)</mo></mrow></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{U}_q\left( \mathfrak{sl}_2 \right)</annotation></semantics></math></span>, in the so-called crystal regime <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi><mo>→</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q \rightarrow 0</annotation></semantics></math></span>. On the other hand, Bougerol and Jeulin showed the appearance of exactly the same Pitman transform in the infinite curvature limit <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>r</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">r \rightarrow \infty</annotation></semantics></math></span> of a Brownian motion on the hyperbolic space <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">H</mi><mn>3</mn></msup><mo>=</mo><mi>S</mi><msub><mi>L</mi><mn>2</mn></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi mathvariant="double-struck">C</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mi>S</mi><msub><mi>U</mi><mn>2</mn></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{H}^3 = SL_2(\mathbb{C})/SU_2</annotation></semantics></math></span>. This paper aims at understanding this phenomenon by giving a unifying point of view. In order to do so, we exhibit a presentation <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi mathvariant="script">U</mi><mi>q</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">ℏ</mi></msubsup><mrow><mo fence="true">(</mo><msub><mrow><mi mathvariant="fraktur">s</mi><mi mathvariant="fraktur">l</mi></mrow><mn>2</mn></msub><mo fence="true">)</mo></mrow></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{U}_q^\hbar\left( \mathfrak{sl}_2 \right)</annotation></semantics></math></span> of the Jimbo-Drinfeld quantum group which isolates the role of curvature <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>r</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">r</annotation></semantics></math></span> and that of the Planck constant <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">ℏ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\hbar</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The simple relationship between parameters is <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi><mo>=</mo><msup><mi>e</mi><mrow><mo>−</mo><mi>r</mi></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q=e^{-r}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The semi-classical limits <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">ℏ</mi><mo>→</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\hbar \rightarrow 0</annotation></semantics></math></span> are the Poisson-Lie groups dual to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>S</mi><msub><mi>L</mi><mn>2</mn></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi mathvariant="double-struck">C</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">SL_2(\mathbb{C})</annotation></semantics></math></span> with varying curvatures <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>r</mi><mo>∈</mo><msub><mi mathvariant="double-struck">R</mi><mo>+</mo></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">r \in \mathbb{R}_+</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We also construct classical and quantum random walks, drawing a full picture which includes Biane's quantum walks and the construction of Bougerol-Jeulin. Taking the curvature parameter <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>r</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">r</annotation></semantics></math></span> to infinity leads indeed to the crystal regime at the level of representation theory (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">ℏ</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\hbar&gt;0</annotation></semantics></math></span>) and to the Bougerol-Jeulin construction in the classical world (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">ℏ</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\hbar=0</annotation></semantics></math></span>). All these results are neatly in accordance with the philosophy of Kirillov's orbit method.</p>{% endraw %}
