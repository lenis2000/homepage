{% raw %}<p>The Erd\"os-Taylor theorem [Acta Math. Acad. Sci. Hungar, 1960] states that if <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="sans-serif">L</mi><mi>N</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathsf{L}_N</annotation></semantics></math></span> is the local time at zero, up to time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>2</mn><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2N</annotation></semantics></math></span>, of a two-dimensional simple, symmetric random walk, then <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mfrac><mi>π</mi><mrow><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi>N</mi></mrow></mfrac><mtext> </mtext><msub><mi mathvariant="sans-serif">L</mi><mi>N</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\tfrac{\pi}{\log N} \,\mathsf{L}_N</annotation></semantics></math></span> converges in distribution to an exponential random variable with parameter one. This can be equivalently stated in terms of the total collision time of two independent simple random walks on the plane. More precisely, if <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi mathvariant="sans-serif">L</mi><mi>N</mi><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>2</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msubsup><mo>=</mo><msubsup><mo>∑</mo><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><mi>N</mi></msubsup><msub><mn>1</mn><mrow><mo stretchy="false">{</mo><msubsup><mi>S</mi><mi>n</mi><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msubsup><mo>=</mo><msubsup><mi>S</mi><mi>n</mi><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>2</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msubsup><mo stretchy="false">}</mo></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathsf{L}_N^{(1,2)}=\sum_{n=1}^N 1_{\{S_n^{(1)}= S_n^{(2)}\}}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, then <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mfrac><mi>π</mi><mrow><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi>N</mi></mrow></mfrac><mtext> </mtext><msubsup><mi mathvariant="sans-serif">L</mi><mi>N</mi><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>2</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msubsup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\tfrac{\pi}{\log N}\, \mathsf{L}^{(1,2)}_N</annotation></semantics></math></span> converges in distribution to an exponential random variable of parameter one. We prove that for every <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>h</mi><mo>≥</mo><mn>3</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">h \geq 3</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the family <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo fence="false" stretchy="true" minsize="1.2em" maxsize="1.2em">{</mo><mfrac><mi>π</mi><mrow><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi>N</mi></mrow></mfrac><mtext> </mtext><msubsup><mi mathvariant="sans-serif">L</mi><mi>N</mi><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>i</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>j</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msubsup><msub><mo fence="false" stretchy="true" minsize="1.2em" maxsize="1.2em">}</mo><mrow><mn>1</mn><mo>≤</mo><mi>i</mi><mo>&lt;</mo><mi>j</mi><mo>≤</mo><mi>h</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex"> \big\{ \frac{\pi}{\log N} \,\mathsf{L}_N^{(i,j)} \big\}_{1\leq i&lt;j\leq h}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, of logarithmically rescaled, two-body collision local times between <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>h</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">h</annotation></semantics></math></span> independent simple, symmetric random walks on the plane converges jointly to a vector of independent exponential random variables with parameter one, thus providing a multivariate version of the Erd\"os-Taylor theorem. We also discuss connections to directed polymers in random environments.</p>{% endraw %}
