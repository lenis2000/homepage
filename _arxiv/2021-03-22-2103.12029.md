{% raw %}<p>There has recently been much activity within the Kardar-Parisi-Zhang universality class spurred by the construction of the canonical limiting object, the parabolic Airy sheet <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">S</mi><mo>:</mo><msup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">R</mi><mn>2</mn></msup><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="double-struck">R</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{S}:\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}</annotation></semantics></math></span> [arXiv:1812.00309]. The parabolic Airy sheet provides a coupling of parabolic Airy<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><mn>2</mn></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_2</annotation></semantics></math></span> processes -- a universal limiting geodesic weight profile in planar last passage percolation models -- and a natural goal is to understand this coupling. Geodesic geometry suggests that the difference of two parabolic Airy<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><mn>2</mn></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_2</annotation></semantics></math></span> processes, i.e., a difference profile, encodes important structural information. This difference profile <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">D</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{D}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, given by <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">R</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="double-struck">R</mi><mo>:</mo><mi>x</mi><mo>↦</mo><mi mathvariant="script">S</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>x</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>−</mo><mi mathvariant="script">S</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>x</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}:x\mapsto \mathcal{S}(1,x)-\mathcal{S}(-1,x)</annotation></semantics></math></span>, was first studied by Basu, Ganguly, and Hammond [arXiv:1904.01717], who showed that it is monotone and almost everywhere constant, with its points of non-constancy forming a set of Hausdorff dimension <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/2</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Noticing that this is also the Hausdorff dimension of the zero set of Brownian motion, we adopt a different approach. Establishing previously inaccessible fractal structure of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">D</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{D}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, we prove, on a global scale, that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">D</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{D}</annotation></semantics></math></span> is absolutely continuous on compact sets to Brownian local time (of rate four) in the sense of increments, which also yields the main result of [arXiv:1904.01717] as a simple corollary. Further, on a local scale, we explicitly obtain Brownian local time of rate four as a local limit of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">D</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{D}</annotation></semantics></math></span> at a point of increase, picked by a number of methods, including at a typical point sampled according to the distribution function <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">D</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{D}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Our arguments rely on the representation of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">S</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{S}</annotation></semantics></math></span> in terms of a last passage problem through the parabolic Airy line ensemble and an understanding of geodesic geometry at deterministic and random times.</p>{% endraw %}
