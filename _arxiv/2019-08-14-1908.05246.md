{% raw %}<p>The Mallows measure is measure on permutations which was introduced by Mallows in connection with ranking problems in statistics. Under this measure, the probability of a permutation <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>π</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\pi</annotation></semantics></math></span> is proportional to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>q</mi><mrow><mi>I</mi><mi>n</mi><mi>v</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>π</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q^{Inv(\pi)}</annotation></semantics></math></span> where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q</annotation></semantics></math></span> is a positive parameter and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>I</mi><mi>n</mi><mi>v</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>π</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Inv(\pi)</annotation></semantics></math></span> is the number of inversions in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>π</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\pi</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We consider the length of the longest common subsequence (LCS) of two independently permutations drawn according to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>μ</mi><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>q</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mu_{n,q}</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>μ</mi><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><msup><mi>q</mi><mo mathvariant="normal" lspace="0em" rspace="0em">′</mo></msup></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mu_{n,q&#x27;}</annotation></semantics></math></span> for some <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><msup><mi>q</mi><mo mathvariant="normal" lspace="0em" rspace="0em">′</mo></msup><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q,q&#x27; &gt;0</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We show that when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>0</mn><mo>&lt;</mo><mi>q</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><msup><mi>q</mi><mo mathvariant="normal" lspace="0em" rspace="0em">′</mo></msup><mo>&lt;</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">0&lt;q,q&#x27;&lt;1</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the limiting law of the LCS is Gaussian. In the regime that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mi>q</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n(1-q) \to \infty</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><msup><mi>q</mi><mo mathvariant="normal" lspace="0em" rspace="0em">′</mo></msup><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n(1-q&#x27;) \to \infty</annotation></semantics></math></span> we show a weak law of large numbers for the LCS. These results extend the results of \cite{Basu} and \cite{Naya} showing weak laws and a limiting law for the distribution of the longest increasing subsequence to showing corresponding results for the longest common subsequence.</p>{% endraw %}
