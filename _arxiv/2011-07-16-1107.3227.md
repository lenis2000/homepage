{% raw %}<p>We introduce a reversible Markovian coagulation-fragmentation process on the set of partitions of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">{</mo><mn>1</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mo>…</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>L</mi><mo stretchy="false">}</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\{1,\ldots,L\}</annotation></semantics></math></span> into disjoint intervals. Each interval can either split or merge with one of its two neighbors. The invariant measure can be seen as the Gibbs measure for a homogeneous pinning model \cite{cf:GBbook}. Depending on a parameter <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>λ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\lambda</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the typical configuration can be either dominated by a single big interval (delocalized phase), or be composed of many intervals of order <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1</annotation></semantics></math></span> (localized phase), or the interval length can have a power law distribution (critical regime). In the three cases, the time required to approach equilibrium (in total variation) scales very differently with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>L</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">L</annotation></semantics></math></span>. In the localized phase, when the initial condition is a single interval of size <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>L</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">L</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the equilibration mechanism is due to the propagation of two "fragmentation fronts" which start from the two boundaries and proceed by power-law jumps.</p>{% endraw %}
