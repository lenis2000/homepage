{% raw %}<p>We determine the scaling limit for permutations conditioned to have longest decreasing subsequence of length at most <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d</annotation></semantics></math></span>. These permutations are also said to avoid the pattern <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>d</mi><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mi>d</mi><mo>⋯</mo><mn>21</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(d+1)d \cdots 2 1</annotation></semantics></math></span> and they can be written as a union of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d</annotation></semantics></math></span> increasing subsequences. We show that these increasing subsequences can be chosen so that, after proper scaling, and centering, they converge in distribution. As the size of the permutations tends to infinity, the distribution of functions generated by the permutations converges to the eigenvalue process of a traceless <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>×</mo><mi>d</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d\times d</annotation></semantics></math></span> Hermitian Brownian bridge.</p>{% endraw %}
