{% raw %}<p>We consider a model of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> non-intersecting squared Bessel processes with one starting point <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>a</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">a&gt;0</annotation></semantics></math></span> at time t=0 and one ending point <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>b</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">b&gt;0</annotation></semantics></math></span> at time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>T</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t=T</annotation></semantics></math></span>. After proper scaling, the paths fill out a region in the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi><mi>x</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">tx</annotation></semantics></math></span>-plane. Depending on the value of the product <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>a</mi><mi>b</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">ab</annotation></semantics></math></span> the region may come to the hard edge at 0, or not. We formulate a vector equilibrium problem for this model, which is defined for three measures, with upper constraints on the first and third measures and an external field on the second measure. It is shown that the limiting mean distribution of the paths at time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t</annotation></semantics></math></span> is given by the second component of the vector that minimizes this vector equilibrium problem. The proof is based on a steepest descent analysis for a <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>4</mn><mo>×</mo><mn>4</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">4 \times 4</annotation></semantics></math></span> matrix valued Riemann-Hilbert problem which characterizes the correlation kernel of the paths at time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We also discuss the precise locations of the phase transitions.</p>{% endraw %}
