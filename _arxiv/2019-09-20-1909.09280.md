{% raw %}<p>Given a chain of groups <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>G</mi><mn>0</mn></msub><mo>≤</mo><msub><mi>G</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo>≤</mo><msub><mi>G</mi><mn>2</mn></msub><mi mathvariant="normal">.</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">.</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">.</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">G_0 \le G_1 \le G_2 ... </annotation></semantics></math></span>, we may form the corresponding chain of their representation rings, together with induction and restriction operators. We may let <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mtext>Res</mtext><mi>l</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\textrm{Res}^l</annotation></semantics></math></span> denote the operator which restricts down <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>l</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">l</annotation></semantics></math></span> steps, and similarly for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mtext>Ind</mtext><mi>l</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\textrm{Ind}^l</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Observe then that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mtext>Ind</mtext><mi>l</mi></msup><msup><mtext>Res</mtext><mi>l</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\textrm{Ind}^l \textrm{Res}^l</annotation></semantics></math></span> is an operator from any particular representation ring to itself. The central question that this paper addresses is: "What happens if the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mtext>Ind</mtext><mi>l</mi></msup><msup><mtext>Res</mtext><mi>l</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\textrm{Ind}^l \textrm{Res}^l</annotation></semantics></math></span> operator is a polynomial in the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mtext>IndRes</mtext></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\textrm{Ind} \textrm{Res}</annotation></semantics></math></span> operator?". We show that chains of wreath products <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">{</mo><msup><mi>H</mi><mi>n</mi></msup><mo>⋊</mo><msub><mi>S</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><msub><mo stretchy="false">}</mo><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>∈</mo><mi mathvariant="double-struck">N</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\{H^n \rtimes S_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}}</annotation></semantics></math></span> have this property, and in particular, the polynomials that appear in the case of symmetric groups are the falling factorial polynomials. An application of this fact gives a remarkable new way to compute characters of wreath products (in particular symmetric groups) using matrix multiplication. We then consider arbitrary chains of groups, and find very rigid constraints that such a chain must satisfy in order for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mtext>Ind</mtext><mi>l</mi></msup><msup><mtext>Res</mtext><mi>l</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\textrm{Ind}^l \textrm{Res}^l</annotation></semantics></math></span> to be a polynomial in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mtext>IndRes</mtext></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\textrm{Ind} \textrm{Res}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Our rigid constraints justify the intuition that this property is indeed a very rare and special property.</p>{% endraw %}
