{% raw %}<p>Consider the normalized adjacency matrices of random <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d</annotation></semantics></math></span>-regular graphs on <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span> vertices with fixed degree <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>≥</mo><mn>3</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d\geq 3</annotation></semantics></math></span>, and denote the eigenvalues as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>λ</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo>=</mo><mi>d</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><msqrt><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow></msqrt><mo>≥</mo><msub><mi>λ</mi><mn>2</mn></msub><mo>≥</mo><msub><mi>λ</mi><mn>3</mn></msub><mo>⋯</mo><mo>≥</mo><msub><mi>λ</mi><mi>N</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\lambda_1=d/\sqrt{d-1}\geq \lambda_2\geq\lambda_3\cdots\geq \lambda_N</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We prove that the optimal (up to an extra <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>N</mi><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">o</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N^{{\rm o}_N(1)}</annotation></semantics></math></span> factor, where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">o</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\rm o}_N(1)</annotation></semantics></math></span> can be arbitrarily small) eigenvalue rigidity holds. More precisely, denote <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>γ</mi><mi>i</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma_i</annotation></semantics></math></span> as the classical location of the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>i</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">i</annotation></semantics></math></span>-th eigenvalue under the Kesten-Mckay law in decreasing order. Then with probability <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><msup><mi>N</mi><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>+</mo><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">o</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1-N^{-1+{\rm o}_N(1)}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, \begin{align*} |\lambda_i-\gamma_i|\leq \frac{N^{{\rm o}_N(1)}}{N^{2/3} (\min\{i,N-i+1\})^{1/3}},\quad \text{ for all } i\in \{2,3,\cdots,N\}. \end{align*} In particular, the fluctuations of extreme eigenvalues are bounded by <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>N</mi><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>2</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn><mo>+</mo><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">o</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N^{-2/3+{\rm o}_N(1)}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. This gives the same order of fluctuation as for the eigenvalues of matrices from the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble.</p>{% endraw %}
