{% raw %}<p>Stochastic Loewner evolution (SLE) is a differential equation driven by a one-dimensional Brownian motion (BM), whose solution gives a stochastic process of conformal transformation on the upper half complex-plane <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">H</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\H</annotation></semantics></math></span>. As an evolutionary boundary of image of the transformation, a random curve (the SLE curve) is generated, which is starting from the origin and running in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">H</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\H</annotation></semantics></math></span> toward the infinity as time is going. The SLE curves provides a variety of statistical ensembles of important fractal curves, if we change the diffusion constant of the driving BM. In the present paper, we consider the Schwarz-Christoffel transformation (SCT), which is a conformal map from <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">H</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\H</annotation></semantics></math></span> to the region <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">H</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\H</annotation></semantics></math></span> with a slit starting from the origin. We prepare a binomial system of SCTs, one of which generates a slit in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">H</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\H</annotation></semantics></math></span> with an angle <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>α</mi><mi>π</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\alpha \pi</annotation></semantics></math></span> from the positive direction of the real axis, and the other of which with an angle <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mi>α</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mi>π</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(1-\alpha) \pi</annotation></semantics></math></span>. One parameter <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>κ</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\kappa &gt;0</annotation></semantics></math></span> is introduced to control the value of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>α</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\alpha</annotation></semantics></math></span> and the length of slit. Driven by a one-dimensional random walk, which is a binomial stochastic process, a random iteration of SCTs is performed. By interpolating tips of slits by straight lines, we have a random path in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">H</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\H</annotation></semantics></math></span>, which we call an Iterative SCT (ISCT) path. It is well-known that, as the number of steps <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span> of random walk goes infinity, each path of random walk divided by <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msqrt><mi>N</mi></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\sqrt{N}</annotation></semantics></math></span> converges to a Brownian curve. Then we expect that the ISCT paths divided by <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msqrt><mi>N</mi></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\sqrt{N}</annotation></semantics></math></span> (the rescaled ISCT paths) converge to the SLE curves in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N \to \infty</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Our numerical study implies that, for sufficiently large <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the rescaled ISCT paths will have the same statistical properties as the SLE curves have, supporting our expectation.</p>{% endraw %}
