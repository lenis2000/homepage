{% raw %}<p>The <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>d</mi><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(d+1)</annotation></semantics></math></span>-dimensional KPZ equation is the canonical model for the growth of rough <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d</annotation></semantics></math></span>-dimensional random surfaces. A deep mathematical understanding of the KPZ equation for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d=1</annotation></semantics></math></span> has been achieved in recent years, and the case <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>≥</mo><mn>3</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d\ge 3</annotation></semantics></math></span> has also seen some progress. The most physically relevant case of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d=2</annotation></semantics></math></span>, however, is not very well-understood mathematically, largely due to the renormalization that is required: in the language of renormalization group analysis, the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d=2</annotation></semantics></math></span> case is neither ultraviolet superrenormalizable like the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d=1</annotation></semantics></math></span> case nor infrared superrenormalizable like the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>≥</mo><mn>3</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d\ge 3</annotation></semantics></math></span> case. Moreover, unlike in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d=1</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the Cole-Hopf transform is not directly usable in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d=2</annotation></semantics></math></span> because solutions to the multiplicative stochastic heat equation are distributions rather than functions. In this article we show the existence of subsequential scaling limits as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ε</mi><mo>→</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\varepsilon \to 0</annotation></semantics></math></span> of Cole-Hopf solutions of the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>2</mn><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(2+1)</annotation></semantics></math></span>-dimensional KPZ equation with white noise mollified to spatial scale <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ε</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\varepsilon</annotation></semantics></math></span> and nonlinearity multiplied by the vanishing factor <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi>ε</mi><msup><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">|\log\varepsilon|^{-1/2}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We also show that the scaling limits obtained in this way do not coincide with solutions to the linearized equation, meaning that the nonlinearity has a non-vanishing effect. We thus propose our scaling limit as a notion of KPZ evolution in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>2</mn><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2+1</annotation></semantics></math></span> dimensions.</p>{% endraw %}
