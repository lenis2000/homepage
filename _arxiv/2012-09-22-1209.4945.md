{% raw %}<p>The article is devoted to the representation theory of locally compact infinite-dimensional group <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">G</mi><mi mathvariant="double-struck">L</mi><mi mathvariant="double-struck">B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{GLB}</annotation></semantics></math></span> of almost upper-triangular infinite matrices over the finite field with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q</annotation></semantics></math></span> elements. This group was defined by S.K., A.V., and Andrei Zelevinsky in 1982 as an adequate <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>=</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n=\infty</annotation></semantics></math></span> analogue of general linear groups <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">G</mi><mi mathvariant="double-struck">L</mi></mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>q</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{GL}(n,q)</annotation></semantics></math></span>. It serves as an alternative to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">G</mi><mi mathvariant="double-struck">L</mi></mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>q</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{GL}(\infty,q)</annotation></semantics></math></span>, whose representation theory is poor. Our most important results are the description of semi-finite unipotent traces (characters) of the group <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">G</mi><mi mathvariant="double-struck">L</mi><mi mathvariant="double-struck">B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{ GLB}</annotation></semantics></math></span> via certain probability measures on the Borel subgroup <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{B}</annotation></semantics></math></span> and the construction of the corresponding von Neumann factor representations of type <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>I</mi><msub><mi>I</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">II_\infty</annotation></semantics></math></span>. As a main tool we use the subalgebra <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">A</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">G</mi><mi mathvariant="double-struck">L</mi><mi mathvariant="double-struck">B</mi></mrow><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal A(\mathbb{ GLB})</annotation></semantics></math></span> of smooth functions in the group algebra <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>L</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">G</mi><mi mathvariant="double-struck">L</mi><mi mathvariant="double-struck">B</mi></mrow><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">L_1(\mathbb{GLB})</annotation></semantics></math></span>. This subalgebra is an inductive limit of the finite--dimensional group algebras <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">C</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">G</mi><mi mathvariant="double-struck">L</mi></mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>q</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\mathbb C}(\mathbb{GL}(n,q))</annotation></semantics></math></span> under parabolic embeddings. As in other examples of the asymptotic representation theory we discover remarkable properties of the infinite case which does not take place for finite groups, like multiplicativity of indecomposable characters or connections to probabilistic concepts. The infinite dimensional Iwahori-Hecke algebra <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="script">H</mi><mi>q</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal H_q(\infty)</annotation></semantics></math></span> plays a special role in our considerations and allows to understand the deep analogy of the developed theory with the representation theory of infinite symmetric group <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>S</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">S(\infty)</annotation></semantics></math></span> which had been intensively studied in numerous previous papers.</p>{% endraw %}
