{% raw %}<p>We consider the double scaling limit for a model of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> non-intersecting squared Bessel processes in the confluent case: all paths start at time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t=0</annotation></semantics></math></span> at the same positive value <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>x</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>a</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">x=a</annotation></semantics></math></span>, remain positive, and are conditioned to end at time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t=1</annotation></semantics></math></span> at <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>x</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">x=0</annotation></semantics></math></span>. After appropriate rescaling, the paths fill a region in the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi><mi>x</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">tx</annotation></semantics></math></span>--plane as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n\to \infty</annotation></semantics></math></span> that intersects the hard edge at <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>x</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">x=0</annotation></semantics></math></span> at a critical time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi><mo>=</mo><msup><mi>t</mi><mo lspace="0em" rspace="0em">∗</mo></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t=t^{*}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. In a previous paper (arXiv:0712.1333), the scaling limits for the positions of the paths at time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi><mo mathvariant="normal">≠</mo><msup><mi>t</mi><mo lspace="0em" rspace="0em">∗</mo></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t\neq t^{*}</annotation></semantics></math></span> were shown to be the usual scaling limits from random matrix theory. Here, we describe the limit as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n\to \infty</annotation></semantics></math></span> of the correlation kernel at critical time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>t</mi><mo lspace="0em" rspace="0em">∗</mo></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t^{*}</annotation></semantics></math></span> and in the double scaling regime. We derive an integral representation for the limit kernel which bears some connections with the Pearcey kernel. The analysis is based on the study of a <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>3</mn><mo>×</mo><mn>3</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">3\times 3</annotation></semantics></math></span> matrix valued Riemann-Hilbert problem by the Deift-Zhou steepest descent method. The main ingredient is the construction of a local parametrix at the origin, out of the solutions of a particular third-order linear differential equation, and its matching with a global parametrix.</p>{% endraw %}
