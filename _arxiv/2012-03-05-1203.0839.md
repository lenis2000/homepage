{% raw %}<p>The distributions of the largest and the smallest eigenvalues of a <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p</annotation></semantics></math></span>-variate sample covariance matrix <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>S</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">S</annotation></semantics></math></span> are of great importance in statistics. Focusing on the null case where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mi>S</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">nS</annotation></semantics></math></span> follows the standard Wishart distribution <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>W</mi><mi>p</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>I</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">W_p(I,n)</annotation></semantics></math></span>, we study the accuracy of their scaling limits under the setting: <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mi>p</mi><mo>→</mo><mi>γ</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n/p\rightarrow \gamma\in(0,\infty)</annotation></semantics></math></span> as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n\rightarrow \infty</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The limits here are the orthogonal Tracy--Widom law and its reflection about the origin. With carefully chosen rescaling constants, the approximation to the rescaled largest eigenvalue distribution by the limit attains accuracy of order <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">O</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mrow><mi>min</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>p</mi><msup><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>2</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn></mrow></msup></mrow><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\mathrm {O}({\min(n,p)^{-2/3}})}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. If <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma&gt;1</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the same order of accuracy is obtained for the smallest eigenvalue after incorporating an additional log transform. Numerical results show that the relative error of approximation at conventional significance levels is reduced by over 50% in rectangular and over 75% in `thin' data matrix settings, even with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>min</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>p</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\min(n,p)</annotation></semantics></math></span> as small as 2.</p>{% endraw %}
