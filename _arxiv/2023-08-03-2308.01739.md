{% raw %}<p>We consider the classic infinite occupancy scheme, where balls are thrown in boxes independently, with probability <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>p</mi><mi>j</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p_j</annotation></semantics></math></span> of hitting box <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>j</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">j</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Each time a box receives its first ball we speak of a record and, more generally, call an <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>r</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">r</annotation></semantics></math></span>-record every event when a box receives its <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>r</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">r</annotation></semantics></math></span>th ball. Assuming that the sequence <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>p</mi><mi>j</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(p_j)</annotation></semantics></math></span> is not decaying too fast, we show that after many balls have been thrown, the suitably scaled point process of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>r</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">r</annotation></semantics></math></span>-record times is approximately Poisson. The joint convergence of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>r</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">r</annotation></semantics></math></span>-record processes is argued under a condition of regular variation.</p>{% endraw %}
