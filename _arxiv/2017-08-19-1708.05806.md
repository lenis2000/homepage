{% raw %}<p>We study the coarsening model (zero-temperature Ising Glauber dynamics) on <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">Z</mi><mi>d</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{Z}^d</annotation></semantics></math></span> (for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>≥</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d \geq 2</annotation></semantics></math></span>) with an asymmetric tie-breaking rule. This is a Markov process on the state space <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">{</mo><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn><msup><mo stretchy="false">}</mo><msup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">Z</mi><mi>d</mi></msup></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\{-1,+1\}^{\mathbb{Z}^d}</annotation></semantics></math></span> of "spin configurations" in which each vertex updates its spin to agree with a majority of its neighbors at the arrival times of a Poisson process. If a vertex has equally many <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">+1</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">-1</annotation></semantics></math></span> neighbors, then it updates its spin value to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">+1</annotation></semantics></math></span> with probability <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">[</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">]</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q \in [0,1]</annotation></semantics></math></span> and to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">-1</annotation></semantics></math></span> with probability <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mo>−</mo><mi>q</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1-q</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The initial state of this Markov chain is distributed according to a product measure with probability <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p</annotation></semantics></math></span> for a spin to be <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">+1</annotation></semantics></math></span>. In this paper, we show that for any given <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p&gt;0</annotation></semantics></math></span>, there exist <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q</annotation></semantics></math></span> close enough to 1 such that a.s. every spin has a limit of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">+1</annotation></semantics></math></span>. This is of particular interest for small values of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p</annotation></semantics></math></span>, for which it is known that if <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q=1/2</annotation></semantics></math></span>, a.s. all spins have a limit of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">-1</annotation></semantics></math></span>. For dimension <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d=2</annotation></semantics></math></span>, we also obtain near-exponential convergence rates for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q</annotation></semantics></math></span> sufficiently large, and for general <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d</annotation></semantics></math></span>, we obtain stretched exponential rates independent of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Two important ingredients in our proofs are refinements of block arguments of Fontes-Schonmann-Sidoravicius and a novel exponential large deviation bound for the Asymmetric Simple Exclusion Process.</p>{% endraw %}
