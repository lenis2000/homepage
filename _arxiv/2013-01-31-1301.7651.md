{% raw %}<p>We first prove that if <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>a</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">a</annotation></semantics></math></span> has a prime factor not dividing <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>b</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">b</annotation></semantics></math></span> then there are infinitely many positive integers <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> such that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo fence="true">(</mo><mfrac linethickness="0px"><mrow><mi>a</mi><mi>n</mi><mo>+</mo><mi>b</mi><mi>n</mi></mrow><mrow><mi>a</mi><mi>n</mi></mrow></mfrac><mo fence="true">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\binom {an+bn} {an}</annotation></semantics></math></span> is not divisible by <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>b</mi><mi>n</mi><mo>+</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">bn+1</annotation></semantics></math></span>. This confirms a recent conjecture of Z.-W. Sun. Moreover, we provide some new divisibility properties of binomial coefficients: for example, we prove that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo fence="true">(</mo><mfrac linethickness="0px"><mrow><mn>12</mn><mi>n</mi></mrow><mrow><mn>3</mn><mi>n</mi></mrow></mfrac><mo fence="true">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\binom {12n} {3n}</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo fence="true">(</mo><mfrac linethickness="0px"><mrow><mn>12</mn><mi>n</mi></mrow><mrow><mn>4</mn><mi>n</mi></mrow></mfrac><mo fence="true">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\binom {12n} {4n}</annotation></semantics></math></span> are divisible by <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>6</mn><mi>n</mi><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">6n-1</annotation></semantics></math></span>, and that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo fence="true">(</mo><mfrac linethickness="0px"><mrow><mn>330</mn><mi>n</mi></mrow><mrow><mn>88</mn><mi>n</mi></mrow></mfrac><mo fence="true">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\binom {330n} {88n}</annotation></semantics></math></span> is divisible by <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>66</mn><mi>n</mi><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">66n-1</annotation></semantics></math></span>, for all positive integers <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We also put forward several related conjectures.</p>{% endraw %}
