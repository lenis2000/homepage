{% raw %}<p>Starting from the Izergin-Korepin 19-vertex model in the quadrant, we introduce two families of rational multivariate functions <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>F</mi><mi>S</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">F_S</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>G</mi><mi>S</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">G_S</annotation></semantics></math></span>; these are in direct analogy with functions introduced by Borodin in the context of the higher-spin 6-vertex model in the quadrant. We prove that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>F</mi><mi>S</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>x</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo separator="true">,</mo><mo>…</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo><msub><mi>x</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mo separator="true">;</mo><mi>z</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">F_S(x_1,\dots,x_N;z)</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>G</mi><mi>S</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>y</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo separator="true">,</mo><mo>…</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo><msub><mi>y</mi><mi>M</mi></msub><mo separator="true">;</mo><mi>z</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">G_S(y_1,\dots,y_M;z)</annotation></semantics></math></span> are symmetric functions in their alphabets <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>x</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo separator="true">,</mo><mo>…</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo><msub><mi>x</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(x_1,\dots,x_N)</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>y</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo separator="true">,</mo><mo>…</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo><msub><mi>y</mi><mi>M</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(y_1,\dots,y_M)</annotation></semantics></math></span>, and pair together to yield a Cauchy identity. Both properties are consequences of the Yang-Baxter equation of the model. We show that, in an appropriate limit of the spectral parameters <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>z</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">z</annotation></semantics></math></span>, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>F</mi><mi>S</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">F_S</annotation></semantics></math></span> tends to a stable symmetric function denoted <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>H</mi><mi>S</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">H_S</annotation></semantics></math></span>. This leads to a simplified version of the Cauchy identity with a fully factorized kernel, and suggests self-duality of the functions <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>H</mi><mi>S</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">H_S</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We obtain a symmetrization formula for the function <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>F</mi><mi>S</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>x</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo separator="true">,</mo><mo>…</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo><msub><mi>x</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mo separator="true">;</mo><mi>z</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">F_S(x_1,\dots,x_N;z)</annotation></semantics></math></span>, which exhibits its symmetry in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>x</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo separator="true">,</mo><mo>…</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo><msub><mi>x</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(x_1,\dots,x_N)</annotation></semantics></math></span>. In contrast to the 6-vertex model, where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi>F</mi><mi>S</mi><mrow><mn>6</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">V</mi></mrow></msubsup><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>x</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo separator="true">,</mo><mo>…</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo><msub><mi>x</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mo separator="true">;</mo><mi>z</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">F^{6{\rm V}}_S(x_1,\dots,x_N;z)</annotation></semantics></math></span> is cast as a sum over the symmetric group <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="fraktur">S</mi><mi>N</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathfrak{S}_N</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the symmetrization formula in the 19-vertex model is over a larger set of objects that we define; we call these objects 2-permutations. As a byproduct of the proof of our symmetrization formula, we obtain explicit formulas for the monodromy matrix elements of the 19-vertex model in a basis that renders them totally spatially symmetric.</p>{% endraw %}
