{% raw %}<p>We consider i.i.d. first-passage percolation (FPP) on the two-dimensional square lattice, in the critical case where edge-weights take the value zero with probability <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mfrac><mn>1</mn><mn>2</mn></mfrac></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\tfrac{1}{2}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Critical FPP is unique in that the Euclidean lengths of geodesics are superlinear -- rather than linear -- in the distance between their endpoints. This fact was speculated by Kesten in 1986 but not confirmed until 2019 by Damron and Tang, who showed a lower bound on geodesic length that is polynomial with degree strictly greater than <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1</annotation></semantics></math></span>. In this paper, we establish the first nontrivial upper bound. Namely, we prove that for a large class of critical edge-weight distributions, the shortest geodesic from the origin to a box of radius <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>R</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">R</annotation></semantics></math></span> uses at most <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>R</mi><mrow><mn>2</mn><mo>+</mo><mi>ϵ</mi></mrow></msup><msub><mi>π</mi><mn>3</mn></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>R</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">R^{2+\epsilon}\pi_3(R)</annotation></semantics></math></span> edges with high probability, for any <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ϵ</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon&gt; 0</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Here <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>π</mi><mn>3</mn></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>R</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\pi_3(R)</annotation></semantics></math></span> is the polychromatic 3-arm probability from classical Bernoulli percolation; upon inserting its conjectural asymptotic, our bound converts to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>R</mi><mrow><mn>4</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>3</mn><mo>+</mo><mi>ϵ</mi></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">R^{4/3 + \epsilon}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. In any case, it is known that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>π</mi><mn>3</mn></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>R</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>≲</mo><msup><mi>R</mi><mrow><mo>−</mo><mi>δ</mi></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\pi_3(R) \lesssim R^{-\delta}</annotation></semantics></math></span> for some <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>δ</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\delta &gt; 0</annotation></semantics></math></span>, so our bound gives an exponent strictly less than <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2</annotation></semantics></math></span>. In the special case of Bernoulli(<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mfrac><mn>1</mn><mn>2</mn></mfrac></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\tfrac{1}{2}</annotation></semantics></math></span>) edge-weights, we replace the additional factor of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>R</mi><mi>ϵ</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">R^\epsilon</annotation></semantics></math></span> with a constant and give an expectation bound.</p>{% endraw %}
