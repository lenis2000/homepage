{% raw %}<p>We consider a polymer with configuration modelled by the trajectory of a Markov chain, interacting with a potential of form <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>u</mi><mo>+</mo><msub><mi>V</mi><mi>n</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">u+V_n</annotation></semantics></math></span> when it visits a particular state 0 at time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span>, with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">{</mo><msub><mi>V</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">}</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\{V_n\}</annotation></semantics></math></span> representing i.i.d. quenched disorder. There is a critical value of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>u</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">u</annotation></semantics></math></span> above which the polymer is pinned by the potential. A particular case not covered in a number of previous studies is that of loop exponent one, in which the probability of an excursion of length <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> takes the form <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ϕ</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\phi(n)/n</annotation></semantics></math></span> for some slowly varying <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ϕ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\phi</annotation></semantics></math></span>; this includes simple random walk in two dimensions. We show that in this case, at all temperatures, the critical values of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>u</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">u</annotation></semantics></math></span> in the quenched and annealed models are equal, in contrast to all other loop exponents, for which these critical values are known to differ, at least at low temperatures.</p>{% endraw %}
