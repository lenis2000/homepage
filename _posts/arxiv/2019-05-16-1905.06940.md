{% raw %}<p>We construct and analyze a continuum dynamical percolation process which evolves in a random environment given by a <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma</annotation></semantics></math></span>-Liouville measure. The homogeneous counterpart of this process describes the scaling limit of discrete dynamical percolation on the rescaled triangular lattice. Our focus here is to study the same limiting dynamics, but where the speed of microscopic updates is highly inhomogeneous in space and is driven by the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma</annotation></semantics></math></span>-Liouville measure associated with a two-dimensional log-correlated field <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>h</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">h</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Roughly speaking, this continuum percolation process evolves very rapidly where the field <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>h</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">h</annotation></semantics></math></span> is high and barely moves where the field <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>h</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">h</annotation></semantics></math></span> is low. Our main results can be summarized as follows. 1. First, we build this inhomogeneous dynamical percolation which we call <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma</annotation></semantics></math></span>-Liouville dynamical percolation (LDP) by taking the scaling limit of the associated process on the triangular lattice. We work with three different regimes each requiring different tools: <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">[</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>2</mn><mo>−</mo><msqrt><mrow><mn>5</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow></msqrt><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma\in [0,2-\sqrt{5/2})</annotation></semantics></math></span>, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">[</mo><mn>2</mn><mo>−</mo><msqrt><mrow><mn>5</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow></msqrt><mo separator="true">,</mo><msqrt><mrow><mn>3</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow></msqrt><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma\in [2-\sqrt{5/2}, \sqrt{3/2})</annotation></semantics></math></span>, and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msqrt><mrow><mn>3</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow></msqrt><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>2</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma\in(\sqrt{3/2},2)</annotation></semantics></math></span>. 2. When <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>&lt;</mo><msqrt><mrow><mn>3</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma&lt;\sqrt{3/2}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, we prove that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma</annotation></semantics></math></span>-LDP is mixing in the Schramm-Smirnov space as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t\to \infty</annotation></semantics></math></span>, quenched in the log-correlated field <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>h</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">h</annotation></semantics></math></span>. On the contrary, when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><msqrt><mrow><mn>3</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma&gt;\sqrt{3/2}</annotation></semantics></math></span> the process is frozen in time. The ergodicity result is a crucial piece of the Cardy embedding project of the second and fourth coauthors, where LDP for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>=</mo><msqrt><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>6</mn></mrow></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma=\sqrt{1/6}</annotation></semantics></math></span> is used to study the scaling limit of a variant of dynamical percolation on uniform triangulations. 3. When <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>γ</mi><mo>&lt;</mo><msqrt><mrow><mn>3</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>4</mn></mrow></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\gamma&lt;\sqrt{3/4}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, we obtain quantitative bounds on the mixing of quad crossing events.</p>{% endraw %}
