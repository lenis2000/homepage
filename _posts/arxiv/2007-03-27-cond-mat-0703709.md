{% raw %}<p>We consider a system of ordered cars moving in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">R</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\R</annotation></semantics></math></span> from right to left. Each car is represented by a point in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">R</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\R</annotation></semantics></math></span>; two or more cars can occupy the same point but cannot overpass. Cars have two possible velocities: either 0 or 1. An unblocked car needs an exponential random time of mean 1 to pass from speed 0 to speed 1 (\emph{slow-to-start}). Car <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>i</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">i</annotation></semantics></math></span>, say, travels at speed 1 until it (possibly) hits the stopped car <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>i</mi><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">i-1</annotation></semantics></math></span> to its left. After the departure of car <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>i</mi><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">i-1</annotation></semantics></math></span>, car <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>i</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">i</annotation></semantics></math></span> waits an exponential random time to change its speed to 1, travels at this speed until it hits again stopped car <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>i</mi><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">i-1</annotation></semantics></math></span> and so on. Initially cars are distributed in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">R</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\R</annotation></semantics></math></span> according to a Poisson process of parameter <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>λ</mi><mo>&lt;</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\lambda&lt;1</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We show that every car will be stopped only a finite number of times and that the final relative car positions is again a Poisson process with parameter <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>λ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\lambda</annotation></semantics></math></span>. To do that, we relate the trajectories of the cars to a <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>M</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mi>M</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">M/M/1</annotation></semantics></math></span> stationary queue as follows. Space in the traffic model is time for the queue. The initial positions of the cars coincide with the arrival process of the queue and the final relative car positions match the departure process of the queue.</p>{% endraw %}
