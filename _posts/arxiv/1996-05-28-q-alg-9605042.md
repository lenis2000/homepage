{% raw %}<p>The classical algebra <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">Λ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\Lambda</annotation></semantics></math></span> of symmetric functions has a remarkable deformation <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi mathvariant="normal">Λ</mi><mo>∗</mo></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\Lambda^*</annotation></semantics></math></span>, which we call the algebra of shifted symmetric functions. In the latter algebra, there is a distinguished basis formed by shifted Schur functions <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi>s</mi><mi>μ</mi><mo>∗</mo></msubsup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">s^*_\mu</annotation></semantics></math></span>, where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>μ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mu</annotation></semantics></math></span> ranges over the set of all partitions. The main significance of the shifted Schur functions is that they determine a natural basis in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>Z</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mrow><mi mathvariant="fraktur">g</mi><mi mathvariant="fraktur">l</mi></mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Z(\frak{gl}(n))</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the center of the universal enveloping algebra <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>U</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mrow><mi mathvariant="fraktur">g</mi><mi mathvariant="fraktur">l</mi></mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">U(\frak{gl}(n))</annotation></semantics></math></span>, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>2</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mo>…</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n=1,2,\ldots</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The functions <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi>s</mi><mi>μ</mi><mo>∗</mo></msubsup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">s^*_\mu</annotation></semantics></math></span> are closely related to the factorial Schur functions introduced by Biedenharn and Louck and further studied by Macdonald and other authors. A part of our results about the functions <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi>s</mi><mi>μ</mi><mo>∗</mo></msubsup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">s^*_\mu</annotation></semantics></math></span> has natural classical analogues (combinatorial presentation, generating series, Jacobi--Trudi identity, Pieri formula). The main application that we have in mind is the asymptotic character theory for the unitary groups <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>U</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">U(n)</annotation></semantics></math></span> and symmetric groups <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>S</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">S(n)</annotation></semantics></math></span> as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n\to\infty</annotation></semantics></math></span>.
