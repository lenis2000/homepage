{% raw %}<p>Interpolation polynomials were introduced by Knop--Sahi in type <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>, and Okounkov in type <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi><mi>C</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">BC</annotation></semantics></math></span>. They are inhomogeneous polynomials whose top terms are Jack and Macdonald polynomials. Thus the expansion coefficients for the product of two interpolation polynomials, known as Littlewood--Richardson coefficients, generalize the corresponding coefficients for Jack/Macdonald polynomials. Special values of interpolation polynomials, known as binomial coefficients, arise in the binomial type expansions of Jack/Macdonald polynomials and Koornwinder polynomials. We prove a number of results for interpolation polynomials and the associated coefficients. These include positivity and monotonicity results for binomial coefficients, partial positivity results for Littlewood--Richardson coefficients, and weighted sum formulas for both kinds of coefficients. As a special case of our results we obtain a new symmetric function inequality, which establishes a ``duality'' between Jack expansion positivity for symmetric functions, and the containment order on partitions, with respect to the shifted basis <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">Ω</mi><mi>λ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn mathvariant="bold">1</mn><mo>+</mo><mi>x</mi><mo separator="true">;</mo><mi>τ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\Omega_\lambda({\bf1}+x;\tau)</annotation></semantics></math></span>, where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn mathvariant="bold">1</mn><mo>=</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mo>…</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\bf1} =(1,\ldots,1)</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">Ω</mi><mi>λ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>x</mi><mo separator="true">;</mo><mi>τ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>=</mo><msub><mi>P</mi><mi>λ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>x</mi><mo separator="true">;</mo><mi>τ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><msub><mi>P</mi><mi>λ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn mathvariant="bold">1</mn><mo separator="true">;</mo><mi>τ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\Omega_\lambda(x;\tau)=P_\lambda(x;\tau)/P_\lambda({\bf1};\tau)</annotation></semantics></math></span> is the normalized Jack polynomial. Our inequality can be seen as an analog of the inequalities of Cuttler--Greene--Skandera+Sra and Khare--Tao, which establish similar dualities between evaluation positivity on the positive orthant, and the dominance and weak dominance orders on partitions, with respect to the normalized Schur basis <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">Ω</mi><mi>λ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>x</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>=</mo><msub><mi>s</mi><mi>λ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>x</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><msub><mi>s</mi><mi>λ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn mathvariant="bold">1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\Omega_\lambda(x)=s_\lambda(x)/s_\lambda({\bf1})</annotation></semantics></math></span> and its shifted version <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">Ω</mi><mi>λ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn mathvariant="bold">1</mn><mo>+</mo><mi>x</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\Omega_\lambda({\bf1}+x)</annotation></semantics></math></span>, respectively. In contrast to our result, the Jack versions of the two latter inequalities, although expected to hold, have not yet been proved.</p>{% endraw %}
