{% raw %}<p>We derive a surprising correspondence between SLE<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><mi>κ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_{\kappa}(\rho)</annotation></semantics></math></span> processes and light cones of the Gaussian free field (GFF). Recall that (one-sided, chordal, origin-seeded) SLE<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><mi>κ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_\kappa(\rho)</annotation></semantics></math></span> processes are in some sense the simplest and most natural variants of the Schramm-Loewner evolution. They were originally defined only for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ρ</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mo>−</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rho &gt; -2</annotation></semantics></math></span>, but one can use L\'evy compensation to extend the definition to any <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ρ</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mo>−</mo><mn>2</mn><mo>−</mo><mfrac><mi>κ</mi><mn>2</mn></mfrac></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rho &gt; -2-\tfrac{\kappa}{2}</annotation></semantics></math></span> and to obtain qualitatively different curves. The triangle <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>T</mi><mo>=</mo><mo stretchy="false">{</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>κ</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>:</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mo>−</mo><mn>2</mn><mo>−</mo><mfrac><mi>κ</mi><mn>2</mn></mfrac><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>∨</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mfrac><mi>κ</mi><mn>2</mn></mfrac><mo>−</mo><mn>4</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>&lt;</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo>&lt;</mo><mo>−</mo><mn>2</mn><mo stretchy="false">}</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T = \{(\kappa, \rho): (-2-\tfrac{\kappa}{2})\vee (\tfrac{\kappa}{2}-4) &lt; \rho &lt; -2 \}</annotation></semantics></math></span> is the primary focus of this paper. When <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>κ</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>∈</mo><mi>T</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(\kappa, \rho) \in T</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the SLE<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><mi>κ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_\kappa(\rho)</annotation></semantics></math></span> curves are highly non-simple (and double points are dense) even though <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>κ</mi><mo>&lt;</mo><mn>4</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\kappa &lt; 4</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Let <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>h</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">h</annotation></semantics></math></span> be an instance of the GFF. Fix <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>κ</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>4</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\kappa\in (0,4)</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>χ</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>2</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><msqrt><mi>κ</mi></msqrt><mo>−</mo><msqrt><mi>κ</mi></msqrt><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\chi = 2/\sqrt{\kappa} - \sqrt{\kappa}/2</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Recall that an imaginary geometry ray is a flow line of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>e</mi><mrow><mi>i</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>h</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mi>χ</mi><mo>+</mo><mi>θ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">e^{i(h/\chi +\theta)}</annotation></semantics></math></span> that looks locally like SLE<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><mi>κ</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_\kappa</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The light cone with parameter <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>θ</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">[</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>π</mi><mo stretchy="false">]</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\theta \in [0, \pi]</annotation></semantics></math></span> is the set of points reachable from the origin by a sequence of rays with angles in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">[</mo><mo>−</mo><mi>θ</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>θ</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>2</mn><mo stretchy="false">]</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">[-\theta/2, \theta/2]</annotation></semantics></math></span>. When <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>θ</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\theta=0</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the light cone looks like SLE<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><mi>κ</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_\kappa</annotation></semantics></math></span>, and when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>θ</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>π</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\theta = \pi</annotation></semantics></math></span> it looks like the range of an SLE<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><mrow><mn>16</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mi>κ</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_{16/\kappa}</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We find that when <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>θ</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>π</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\theta \in (0, \pi)</annotation></semantics></math></span> the light cones are either fractal carpets with a dense set of holes or space-filling regions with no holes. We show that every non-space-filling light cone (with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>θ</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>π</mi><mo stretchy="false">]</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\theta \in (0,\pi]</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>κ</mi><mo>∈</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>0</mn><mo separator="true">,</mo><mn>4</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\kappa \in (0,4)</annotation></semantics></math></span>) agrees in law with the range of an SLE<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><mi>κ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_\kappa(\rho)</annotation></semantics></math></span> process with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>κ</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>∈</mo><mi>T</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(\kappa, \rho) \in T</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Conversely, the range of any SLE<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><mi>κ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_\kappa(\rho)</annotation></semantics></math></span> with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>κ</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>∈</mo><mi>T</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(\kappa,\rho) \in T</annotation></semantics></math></span> agrees in law with a non-space-filling light cone. As a consequence, we obtain the first proof that these SLE<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mrow></mrow><mi>κ</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_\kappa(\rho)</annotation></semantics></math></span> processes are continuous and show that they are natural path-valued functions of the GFF.</p>{% endraw %}
