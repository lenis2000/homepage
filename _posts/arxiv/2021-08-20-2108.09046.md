{% raw %}<p>In the present work, we study the two-dimensional anisotropic KPZ equation (AKPZ), which is formally given by \begin{equation*} \partial_t h=\tfrac12 \Delta h + \lambda ((\partial_1 h)^2)-(\partial_2 h)^2) +\xi\,, \end{equation*} where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ξ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\xi</annotation></semantics></math></span> denotes a space-time white noise and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>λ</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\lambda&gt;0</annotation></semantics></math></span> is the so-called coupling constant. The AKPZ equation is a {\it critical} SPDE, meaning that not only it is analytically ill-posed but also the breakthrough path-wise techniques for singular SPDEs [M. Hairer, Ann. Math. 2014] and [M. Gubinelli, P. Imkeller and N. Perkowski, Forum of Math., Pi, 2015] are not applicable. As shown in [G. Cannizzaro, D. Erhard, F. Toninelli, arXiv, 2020], the equation regularised at scale <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span> has a diffusion coefficient that diverges logarithmically as the regularisation is removed in the limit <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N\to\infty</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Here, we study the \emph{weak coupling limit} where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>λ</mi><mo>=</mo><msub><mi>λ</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mover accent="true"><mi>λ</mi><mo>^</mo></mover><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><msqrt><mrow><mi>log</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi>N</mi></mrow></msqrt></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\lambda=\lambda_N=\hat\lambda/\sqrt{\log N}</annotation></semantics></math></span>: this is the correct scaling that guarantees that the nonlinearity has a still non-trivial but non-divergent effect. In fact, as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N\to\infty</annotation></semantics></math></span> the sequence of equations converges to the linear stochastic heat equation \begin{equation*} \partial_t h =\tfrac{\nu_{\rm eff}}{2} \Delta h + \sqrt{\nu_{\rm eff}}\xi\,, \end{equation*} where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>ν</mi><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">e</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">f</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">f</mi></mrow></msub><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\nu_{\rm eff} &gt;1</annotation></semantics></math></span> is explicit and depends non-trivially on <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mover accent="true"><mi>λ</mi><mo>^</mo></mover></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\hat\lambda</annotation></semantics></math></span>. This is the first full renormalization-type result for a critical, singular SPDE which cannot be linearised via Cole-Hopf or any other transformation.</p>{% endraw %}
