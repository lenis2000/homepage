{% raw %}<p>The Branching Brownian Motions (BBM) are particles performing independent Brownian motions in <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="double-struck">R</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb R</annotation></semantics></math></span> and each particle at rate 1 creates a new particle at her current position; the newborn particle increments and branchings are independent of the other particles. The <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span>-BBM starts with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span> particles and at each branching time, the leftmost particle is removed so that the total number of particles is <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span> for all times. The <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span>-BBM was proposed by Maillard and belongs to a family of processes introduced by Brunet and Derrida. We fix a density <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ρ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rho</annotation></semantics></math></span> with a left boundary <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>L</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>sup</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mo stretchy="false">{</mo><mi>r</mi><mo>∈</mo><mi mathvariant="double-struck">R</mi><mo>:</mo><msubsup><mo>∫</mo><mi>r</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></msubsup><mi>ρ</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>x</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mi>d</mi><mi>x</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">}</mo><mo>&gt;</mo><mo>−</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">L=\sup\{r\in\mathbb R: \int_r^\infty \rho(x)dx=1\}&gt;-\infty</annotation></semantics></math></span> and let the initial particle positions be iid continuous random variables with density <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ρ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rho</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We show that the empirical measure associated to the particle positions at a fixed time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t</annotation></semantics></math></span> converges to an absolutely continuous measure with density <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ψ</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mo>⋅</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\psi(\cdot,t)</annotation></semantics></math></span>, as <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N\to\infty</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The limit <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>ψ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\psi</annotation></semantics></math></span> is solution of a free boundary problem (FBP) when this solution exists. The existence of solutions for finite time-intervals has been recently proved by Lee.</p>{% endraw %}
