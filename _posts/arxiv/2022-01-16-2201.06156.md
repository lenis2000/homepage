{% raw %}<p>We show that the counts of low degree irreducible factors of a random polynomial <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>f</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">f</annotation></semantics></math></span> over <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="double-struck">F</mi><mi>q</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{F}_q</annotation></semantics></math></span> with independent but non-uniform coefficients behave like that of a uniform random polynomial, exhibiting a form of universality for random polynomials over finite fields. Our strongest results require various assumptions on the parameters, but we are able to obtain results requiring only <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>q</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>p</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">q=p</annotation></semantics></math></span> a prime with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi><mo>≤</mo><mi>exp</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msup><mi>n</mi><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mn>13</mn></mrow></msup><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p\leq \exp({n^{1/13}})</annotation></semantics></math></span> where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span> is the degree of the polynomial. Our proofs use Fourier analysis, and rely on tools recently applied by Breuillard and Varj\'u to study the <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>a</mi><mi>x</mi><mo>+</mo><mi>b</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">ax+b</annotation></semantics></math></span> process, which show equidistribution for <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>f</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>α</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">f(\alpha)</annotation></semantics></math></span> at a single point. We extend this to handle multiple roots and the Hasse derivatives of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>f</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">f</annotation></semantics></math></span>, which allow us to study the irreducible factors with multiplicity.</p>{% endraw %}
