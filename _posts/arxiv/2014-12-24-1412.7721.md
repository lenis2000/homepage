{% raw %}<p>This paper develops a method to carry out the large-<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span> asymptotic analysis of a class of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span>-dimensional integrals arising in the context of the so-called quantum separation of variables method. We push further ideas developed in the context of random matrices of size <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span>, but in the present problem, two scales <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><msup><mi>N</mi><mi>α</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/N^{\alpha}</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/N</annotation></semantics></math></span> naturally occur. In our case, the equilibrium measure is <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>N</mi><mi>α</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N^{\alpha}</annotation></semantics></math></span>-dependent and characterised by means of the solution to a <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>2</mn><mo>×</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2\times 2</annotation></semantics></math></span> Riemann--Hilbert problem, whose large-<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span> behavior is analysed in detail. Combining these results with techniques of concentration of measures and an asymptotic analysis of the Schwinger-Dyson equations at the distributional level, we obtain the large-<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span> behavior of the free energy explicitly up to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>o</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">o(1)</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The use of distributional Schwinger-Dyson is a novelty that allows us treating sufficiently differentiable interactions and the mixing of scales <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><msup><mi>N</mi><mi>α</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/N^{\alpha}</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/N</annotation></semantics></math></span>, thus waiving the analyticity assumptions often used in random matrix theory.</p>{% endraw %}
